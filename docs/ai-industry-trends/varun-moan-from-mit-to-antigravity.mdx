## Career arc: systems → autonomy → AI products

### MIT and research taste

Varun describes MIT as centered on <strong>systems</strong> and <strong>performance engineering</strong> rather than classical ML coursework. He worked in <strong>PDOS</strong> (Parallel and Distributed Operating Systems) with faculty including <strong>Sam Madden</strong>, completed a <strong>SuperUROP</strong>, and did his <strong>MEng</strong> with Madden—much of it around <strong>databases</strong> and systems. He was a two-time TA for <strong>6.172</strong> (performance engineering) and emphasizes enjoying <em>making things fast</em>.

### After MIT: robotics and long iteration cycles

He joined <strong>Nuro</strong> (self-driving / autonomous delivery) for about <strong>four years</strong>. He reflects that <strong>robotics hype cycles</strong> recur every few years, but <strong>productionization</strong> took a long time—heavy <strong>edge cases</strong> for self-driving—and that real scale (e.g. Waymo) rested on <em>many years</em> of prior work.

### Cultural signal: Torvalds and “vibe coding”

He cites <strong>Linus Torvalds</strong> recently building a repo with Antigravity as a <em>directional</em> anecdote—not purely as product marketing, but as evidence that even someone strongly associated with traditional editor workflows may <strong>skip autocomplete → skip chat → go straight to agentic coding</strong> when the surface is compelling.

## Why capabilities jumped: it is not “just scale”

### SWE-bench as a concrete scoreboard

  <p className="m-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
    <strong>SWE-bench</strong> (roughly): ~500 real issues from popular repos; model proposes a patch; success if <strong>golden unit tests</strong> pass.
  </p>

| Era (approx.) | Sentiment | Score |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Early 2024 (e.g. around <strong>Devin</strong> announcement) | Very excited, sometimes <em>overclaimed</em> full automation | ~<strong>15%</strong> |
| Current discussion | Benchmark largely “cracked,” with caveats | <strong>&gt; 80%</strong> |

He argues part of the remaining gap is <strong>ill-posed tasks</strong>: vague issue statements, or tests that are not themselves high quality—so the ceiling is not purely “model failure.”

### Compute efficiency (informal)

Labs talk about <strong>compute efficiency</strong>: *what one FLOP buys today vs a year ago*. A loose way to write the *idea* (not a literal physical law)—think of an informal “capability per FLOP” term increasing over time:

$$
E_t \;\gg\; E_{t-\Delta}
\quad\text{(read } E_t \text{ as useful intelligence per unit compute at time } t\text{).}
$$

He compares this mindset to <strong>Moore’s law</strong> / Intel’s <strong>tick‑tock</strong>: not one silver bullet, but <strong>many compounding improvements</strong> (process, microarchitecture, etc.—here: data, algorithms, RL, tooling).

## Biggest gaps and open problems

### Continuous learning vs “running forever without growing”

Models do not yet learn like a human doing <strong>deduction → sleep → deeper insight the next day</strong>. Operating longer does not obviously increase the underlying <strong>knowledge / value function</strong> unless you add mechanisms (distillation, retrieval, dynamic weights, etc.). He floats analogies: different companies (Meta vs Tesla) reward different behaviors—maybe internal <strong>objectives</strong> of an assistant must <em>change</em> with context, not only prompts.

### Asking the “hard questions”

Even with strong retrieval and human-level deductions <em>within known science</em>, he feels systems rarely produce the <em>one transformative question</em> that unlocks the hardest problems—related to views he has heard from <strong>Demis Hassabis</strong> and more conservative <strong>AGI timelines</strong> (~5–10 years) vs some corporate marketing claims.

---

## Collaboration: the “water cooler” problem

Human teams share <strong>tacit context</strong>—coffee chats, undocumented assumptions. Models only see what is <strong>digitized and retrieved</strong>. His practical example: a Google Doc for running <strong>evals</strong> can still be <em>underspecified</em> relative to what the team actually knows; fixing that (making specs <strong>machine-readable</strong>) is worth it when automation value is high.

He expects <strong>high-value domains</strong> will invest in feeding agents better context; niche or new domains may stay “disadvantaged” like <em>someone never invited to informal conversations</em>.

### Software vs “hard sciences”

Software benefits from <strong>cheap iteration</strong> (run tests, try variants). Hard sciences have <strong>long loop times</strong> and huge tacit knowledge in researchers’ heads—so AI adoption curves may diverge: faster in the <strong>bits</strong> world than the <strong>atoms</strong> world.

---

## Academia, hiring, and what students should know

Google still hires <strong>new MIT grads</strong>; the bar has shifted: <strong>you must use these tools</strong> to stay productive. The strongest pattern is <em>both</em> <strong>fundamentals</strong> <em>and</em> fluent tool use—fundamentals-only beats tool-only, but tools are table stakes.

Fundamentals matter for <strong>judgment</strong>: models make <strong>trade-offs</strong> without your unspoken constraints (“rewrite module X” does not mean “rewrite the universe”). For <strong>fundamental research</strong>, he is skeptical models replace researchers <em>today</em>, but sees big <strong>boilerplate acceleration</strong>.

---

## Five years out: the developer workday

- <strong>Higher productivity expectations</strong>; one engineer multiplexing on the order of <strong>10–20 agents</strong> (some long-running).
- Need <strong>good UIs</strong> to check in on agents—avoid three-month black boxes that drift from intent (like any large human project).
- Software work decomposes into <strong>what to build</strong>, <strong>how to build</strong>, and <strong>building</strong>; <em>more time on “what”</em> because product surface and user attention are bottlenecks—similar to past jumps in abstraction (assembly → high-level languages).
