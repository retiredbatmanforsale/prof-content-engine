# Hallucinations in large language models

When a model answers in **clear, fluent paragraphs**, it is easy to forget what is actually happening under the hood. This lesson stays at the level you already have: **probability**, **randomness**, and **language**—without diving into equations. The goal is to **recognize** hallucination, **name** it, and **use prompts** where they can genuinely help.

## What people mean by “hallucination” here

In everyday speech, **hallucination** in LLMs usually means: the model produced something that is **wrong, invented, or unsupported**—yet it is phrased as if it were **sure**. That is different from saying “I don’t know” or “I would need to check a database.”

| Everyday label | What often happened |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **Hallucination** | Plausible continuation that **was not grounded** in reliable evidence for this question |
| **Honest uncertainty** (rare unless prompted) | Hedged language or a request for more context |

**Bridging to prompts:** you cannot “patch” mathematics you never showed the model, but you **can** change the **task shape**: require **sources**, **step visibility**, **forbidden invention**, or a **second pass** that only critiques. Prompt design does not remove randomness—it **steers** behavior and **surfaces** risk.

---

## Not a database—a next-token engine

An LLM is **not** a lookup table of facts. It is a **large probabilistic model**: at each step it assigns **chances** to many possible next pieces of text, then **samples** (or picks sharply) among them. **Stochastic** means: small changes in temperature or in the prompt can change the path—and **plausible** wrong answers sit beside **plausible** right ones in that space.

## Ways things go wrong (without blaming “bugs”)

These labels help teams discuss failures calmly—each suggests **different** mitigations.

| Mode | Plain description | Simple example shape |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Fabrication** | Names, dates, citations, or “studies” that **read well** but were **never provided** | “According to a 2019 paper by…” with no real paper in context |
| **Inaccurate reasoning** | Steps **sound** logical but a link in the chain is wrong | Correct premises, wrong conclusion; or algebra slip hidden in words |
| **Outdated or contaminated knowledge** | Training mix included **old** facts or **noisy** text; the model **echoes** that prior | Policy numbers from years ago; rare edge cases over-represented in data |
| **Overgeneralization** | Smooth, **abstract** answer that **erases nuance** | “Always do X” when the truth is “it depends on jurisdiction / patient / market” |

None of these require malice or “broken code.” They arise from **how** the system is built and **what** it was trained to optimize (coherent language, not your personal liability line).

## When fuzzy output is almost OK—and when it is not

| Zone | Why hallucination might be **more tolerable** | What you still do |
|------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| **Brainstorming, ideation, tone drafts** | You will **edit**, merge, or throw away most lines | Label ideas as **draft**; rank or vote; human picks |
| **Legal, medical, finance, research citations** | Errors can **harm** people, contracts, or science | **Sources**, **human sign-off**, **systems** designed for evidence—not vibes |

The model does not know which zone you are in unless **you** say so in the prompt and in your **workflow**.

## Leveraging prompts to **control** and **mitigate** (not erase)

Prompting cannot **guarantee** truth. It can **narrow** the model’s job and **raise the cost** of silent invention.

| Technique | Idea in one line |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Grounding** | Paste **allowed** text (policy, spec, transcript) and say: “Answer **only** from this; if missing, write **‘not in source’**.” |
| **Constraints** | “**No** external citations,” “**max** three claims,” “every claim ends with **[source: section]”** |
| **Structure** | Force **bullet evidence** before a summary, or JSON with a **confidence** field the human must interpret cautiously |
| **Scope** | “Do **not** infer law; describe **general** patterns only” or “I am not asking for diagnosis—**education** only” |
| **Two-pass verification** | Generate as one **role**, then **critique** as another (below) |

## “Expert A” then “Expert B”—critique as a lever

A practical pattern from the field: LLMs are often **stronger at checking** a given piece of text than at **inventing** a perfect first draft from thin air—especially if you give them **the same** material to audit.

**Pass 1 — Expert A (generator):**  
“Draft recommendations for \<goal\> using **only** the pasted brief.”

**Pass 2 — Expert B (critic, separate call or clear section):**  
“You are a **skeptical reviewer**. List **every** claim in the draft that is not directly supported by the brief. Flag **logical gaps**. Do **not** rewrite the whole doc—output a **findings list**.”

| Why two hats help | Caveat |
|-------------------|--------|
| Critique mode encourages **“what could be wrong?”** | A second LLM pass can still **miss** errors or **invent** new ones—use it as **assist**, not **oracle** |

---

## Hallucination is expected behavior—not a switch you can turn off

Because the core is **probabilistic** and **stochastic**, you should plan for **residual risk**: sometimes the model will still **fill gaps** beautifully and wrongly. Your job shifts from **elimination** to **management**: **process**, **tools** (retrieval, calculators, APIs), **prompts**, and **humans** in the loop where stakes are high.
