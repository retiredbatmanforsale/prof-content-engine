Most software engineers would be navigating AI **wrong**.

As the utility and spread of **generative AI platforms and tools** keep growing, the phrase **“education on AI”** is too often collapsed into **knowing how to use AI tools**. That shortcut feels efficient—but it quietly redefines what it means to be an **AI** or **ML engineer**.

**Can someone really be called an AI or ML engineer if they only know how to use AI tools?**

---

## The tool-first story—and what leaders at the frontier actually believe

The **tool-first** narrative goes like this: code generation is democratized; anyone can ship snippets; **traditional computer science**, **algorithms**, and **machine learning fundamentals** matter less.

If you listen to how **Demis Hassabis**, **Andrej Karpathy**, **Ilya Sutskever**, **Yann LeCun**, and **Jeff Dean** talk about the field, you hear the **opposite**: in a high-velocity, tool-flooded ecosystem, **deep foundational knowledge** is not a legacy requirement—it is a **primary differentiator** and a **competitive advantage** for elite engineers.

---

## History is the honest mirror

History is the cleanest way to form an opinion about **engineering shifts**. The arc of programming has always been **toward higher abstraction**: hide more of the “how and why” of the layer below so you can focus on **outcomes**. Every few years we climb another rung.

That climb created enormous leverage—and it never removed the need to **understand what was being abstracted**.

### The 1950s: hand-to-hand combat with hardware

In the mid-20th century, programming meant **machine code**: binary or hex, opcodes, memory addresses, jump targets computed by hand. There was little separation between **tool** and **fundamental**; the work was a **priesthood** of people with access and stamina.

**Assembly** added mnemonics (`MOV`, `ADD`, `XCHG`) but stayed **machine-dependent**: registers, alignment, instruction sets—still intimate with the processor.

### Compilers and the first real abstraction leap

The 1950s also gave us **Fortran** (Formula Translation): write math-like expressions such as `x = a + b * c` and let the machine map them down.

**Compilers**—translators from high-level intent to machine code—met the same objections you hear about **AI-assisted coding** today:

| Objection then | Echo today |
|------------------|------------|
| **Efficiency** — compiled code could never match careful assembly | “AI code will always be bloated or slow” |
| **Control** — if you do not “code to the metal,” you cannot debug | “If I did not write it, I cannot trust it” |
| **Trust** — how do you know the machine honored intent? | “How do I know the model understood me?” |

By **1958**, more than half of the code on **IBM** machines was **Fortran-generated**. The tool won because engineers **learned to trust it while still understanding the layer beneath**.

### What that teaches us

The engineers who won were not the ones who **rejected** the compiler; they were the ones who **mastered high-level logic** while keeping enough **grounding** to reason about **optimization, bugs, and failure modes**. Fundamentals did not vanish—they **moved up a level**: from address fiddling to **algorithms and problem decomposition**.

## Progression of abstractions (where we sit now)

| Era | Abstraction | You mostly manipulate | Fundamentals that still bite | Tooling |
|-----|-------------|------------------------|------------------------------|---------|
| **1940s–50s** | Machine code | Binary / hex strings | Registers, addresses | Punch cards, wiring |
| **1950s–60s** | Assembly | Mnemonics | ISA, alignment | Assemblers |
| **1960s–80s** | High-level (C, Pascal) | Structured logic | Data structures, pointers, memory | Optimizing compilers |
| **1990s–2010s** | 4GL / scripting (e.g. Python) | OO / functional patterns | Libraries, APIs, packaging | IDEs, debuggers, package managers |
| **2020s+** | **AI-assisted / intent-driven** | Prompts, specs, constraints | **Systems design, ML, DL**, evaluation | LLMs, copilots, agents |

We are in the **intent** layer: describe outcomes and constraints; let compilers, frameworks, and models **plan** implementation. That does **not** remove understanding—it **raises the bar**: you must judge **correctness**, **failure modes**, and **hallucinated plausibility** the way a Fortran author had to reason about **numerical stability** and **algorithmic intent**.

## Three actionable pillars as you step in

### Pillar 1 — Depth on principles beats hype velocity

A few years from now, nobody will remember **which model launched this week**. They will remember the engineers who knew **what was actually going on**.

You will feel pressure to chase every benchmark and release. **Resist the default.** Interfaces churn; **principles move slowly**. Durable engineers understand—at least directionally—**tokens, embeddings, optimization, transformer mechanics**, and how **data and compute** quietly set the ceiling. They also respect **open problems**: causal reasoning, world models, continual learning.

Do not confuse this with **theory-only** cosplay. Modern AI is **messy and empirical**. You will not always know why one prompt works. You will ship systems that behave for reasons you cannot fully prove. **That is normal.**

- **First principles** → compression and clarity in decisions.  
- **Experimentation** → ground truth about what works in *your* stack.  
- Over time you develop **taste**—and taste matters more when **everyone** has the same tools.

We are not building **models as endpoints** anymore; we are building **systems** where models are **components**. Leverage is in **stitching**: retrieval, tools, memory, orchestration, **evaluation loops**. For traditional SWEs, **software architecture** matters more than ever.

**Moore’s law** still whispers: compute tends to get **cheaper** and systems **faster** on a cadence. The winners are not those who **wait** for better tools—they learn to **use today’s models and tools better than anyone else**.

### Pillar 2 — Ship solid software *on top of* these abstractions

You do not always need to **train from scratch**; you need to know **when not to**. You will call **APIs**, compose **services**, and ship products on layers that did not exist a few years ago—same pattern as assembly → languages → frameworks.

Your job is not blindly **one level up or down**.

Your job is to **choose the right abstraction**.

Scope expansion is **easier** now—use that to **see the system**: UX, backend, model behavior, distributed design—not only a narrow badge title.

### Pillar 3 — Evaluation is the discipline that separates you

Building is **easy**; you can ship in a day. **Knowing whether it works**—reliably, under load, on real distributions—is **hard**. That is where serious engineers outrun **vibe-only** shipping.

Push **evaluation** across **datasets, use cases, scale, functionality, load, stress**—and study **failure modes** with the same rigor you would for any production service.

## A closing line to keep on your desk

> I am a true artist if I can start from first principles, learn the best-known art, and reimplement it in a way that has never been done before.
