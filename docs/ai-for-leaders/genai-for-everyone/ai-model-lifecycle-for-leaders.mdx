Whether the system is **classical machine learning**, **deep learning**, or **generative AI**, teams still talk about the same object: a **model**—something you **train**, then **query** at **inference** time. The vocabulary changes; the **shape of the work** is surprisingly stable.

This lesson is a **single map** of that work, so you can read roadmaps, vendor decks, and risk reviews without getting lost in equations.

---

## Two moments in every model’s life

| Stage | Plain meaning | What you fund / govern |
|--------|----------------|-------------------------|
| **Training** | The model **learns** from historical examples | Data rights, compute budgets, research time, reproducibility |
| **Inference** | The model **answers** new questions in production | Latency, uptime, monitoring, human oversight |

**Complexity** is not magic—it tracks **how many adjustable knobs** the architecture has: a tiny model might have **a handful of parameters**; a frontier network might have **billions**. More knobs can mean more capacity—and more ways to **overfit** or **misbehave** if data and process are weak.

---

## Where “model” sits in the AI landscape

```text
  Artificial intelligence  ─────────────────────────────►  broad goal
        └── Machine learning  ───────────────────────►  learn from data
              └── Deep learning  ─────────────────────►  deep stacks of learned features
                    └── Generative AI  ───────────────►  outputs text, images, audio…
```

A **model** is the concrete artifact: parameters + architecture + training recipe. It always lives **inside** one of those traditions—even when marketing says only “AI.”

## 1 · Gather and lock down data

Every serious model starts as a **data project**. Example from your world: a vision system at an **airport** that must tell **human faces** from **animals** at a gate needs **many thousands** of curated examples—not a handful of screenshots.

| Activity | Why it shows up on a budget line |
|----------|----------------------------------|
| **Acquisition** | Buy datasets, commission capture, partner with operators, or (carefully) use **synthetic** data |
| **Rights & consent** | Legal basis to train and to run inference in each region |
| **Labeling** | Humans or tools assigning the “right answer” per image, document, or row |

## 2 · Clean, balance, and engineer the data

**Cleaning** makes examples usable: remove **corrupt files**, fix **blank fields**, clip wild **outliers**, standardize **formats** and **sizes** (especially for images and documents).

**Data engineering** handles **distribution**: if “human face” photos overwhelm “animal” photos, the model can learn to **always guess the majority class**. Teams fix imbalance with **resampling**, **re-weighting**, or **augmentation** (carefully—augmentation should respect physics and privacy).

| Scale | Storage & access pattern |
|-------|---------------------------|
| **Notebook-scale proof of concept** | A laptop, a CSV, a folder of images |
| **Production-scale** | Object stores, lakehouses, streaming pipelines, **governed** feature stores |

## 3 · Choose architecture and loss (still without code)

**Model equation** is jargon for “what computation turns inputs into outputs.” **Linear regression** is one equation; a **deep neural network** nests many small equations—hence the term **architecture**.

**Loss function** is the scoreboard during training: the system **nudges parameters** to make that score better.

| Problem type | Output you expect | Typical loss (names only) |
|----------------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **Regression** | A number on the real line (e.g. next week’s **price level**) | Mean squared error family |
| **Binary classification** | Two classes (e.g. **spam / not spam**) | Cross-entropy style losses |
| **Multi-class** | Many labels (emotions, product codes) | Cross-entropy generalized |

**Evaluation** must match the job: **accuracy**, **sensitivity**, **specificity** for many classification settings; **R²** and error metrics for regression. The right metric is the one that mirrors **business harm**—the same way a sales org tracks **pipeline** while finance tracks **run-rate savings.**

## Probabilities at a glance (sigmoid vs softmax)

## 4 · Train, watch for overfit, then validate

Training is a loop in words:

1. **Forward pass** — show the model an example; read its guess.  
2. **Loss** — measure how wrong the guess is.  
3. **Backward pass** — adjust knobs to reduce that loss next time.  
4. **Repeat** across **epochs** — you should see loss **trend down** on healthy runs (your teams will show you the curve).

**Overfitting** means the model **memorized the training set** but **fails on unseen data**. Good generalization shows **similar quality** on train and test when both are drawn fairly from the same world.

:::tip[What to ask in a review]

“If we plot train vs test metrics side by side, **where do they diverge**—and what did we change in the recipe right before that?”

:::

## 5 · Deploy, observe, and feed learning back

**Deployment** is not a PDF—it is **software**: containers, APIs on **GCP / AWS / Azure**, batch jobs, or edge devices. An **API endpoint** lets the airport operations team send live frames and receive **structured decisions** (counts, alerts, confidence bands).

**Feedback** closes the loop: field teams report **false alarms**, **missed animals**, **lighting issues**. That feedback becomes **new labels**, **policy tweaks**, or **retraining**—which sends you back to **data** and **governance**.

---

## Same pipeline, generative twist

For **generation**, the architecture is tuned to **predict the next token** (or patch, or frame) autoregressively—but the **business stages** still line up: **data → train → evaluate → ship → monitor → improve**.

## Closing

You do not need to derive backpropagation to **govern** AI programs. You **do** need to recognize the **pipeline**: data quality and rights at the base, honest **evaluation** in the middle, and **operational feedback** at the top. When a vendor says “the model is done,” ask which **stages** above they are skipping—and what risk that pushes onto **your** organization.
