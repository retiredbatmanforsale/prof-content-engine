In the previous section, we established the <strong>importance of modeling sequential dependencies</strong>. We now turn to how the field historically addressed this problem, beginning with a shift from traditional <strong>feedforward architectures</strong> to networks capable of handling <em>temporal dynamics</em>.

---

## Revisiting the perceptron

Let us return briefly to the basic building block introduced in <strong>Lecture 1</strong>: the <strong>perceptron</strong>. Each perceptron takes a vector of inputs <strong>x₁, x₂, …, x<sub>m</sub></strong>, applies a <strong>weighted sum</strong>, and passes the result through a <strong>non-linear activation function</strong> to produce an output <strong>y</strong>. This output can then feed into further layers to construct deep networks.

      loading="lazy"
      decoding="async"
    />
  </div>
</div>

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-xl border-2 border-slate-300 bg-gradient-to-br from-slate-50 to-indigo-50/40 p-5 dark:border-slate-600 dark:from-slate-900 dark:to-indigo-950/30">
  <p className="m-0 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">Vector form</p>
  $$
  y = \phi\left( \sum_{i=1}^{m} w_i x_i + b \right) = \phi(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b)
  $$
  <p className="mb-0 mt-2 text-center text-xs text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">
    <strong>ϕ</strong> — activation · <strong>w<sub>i</sub></strong>, <strong>b</strong> — learnable parameters
  </p>
</div>

In its basic form, the perceptron operates on a <strong>fixed-length vector</strong> with <strong>no awareness</strong> of any sequential structure. All inputs are assumed to belong to a <strong>single time slice</strong>{' '}
<span className="whitespace-nowrap rounded-md border border-amber-400 bg-amber-100 px-2 py-0.5 text-xs font-bold text-amber-950 dark:border-amber-600 dark:bg-amber-950/50 dark:text-amber-100">
  STATIC SINGLE STEP
</span>
. There is <strong>no notion of TIME</strong> or temporal ordering or dependence on previous data points. This limitation makes such architectures <em>insufficient</em> for handling sequential data.

---

## Applying feedforward networks to time series: a naive attempt

One <strong>naive</strong> way to apply a static neural network to sequence data is to treat <strong>each time step independently</strong>. Imagine rotating our input-output diagram vertically, where each input <strong>x<sub>t</sub></strong> is processed by the <strong>same network</strong> <strong>f</strong> to yield an output <strong>y<sub>t</sub></strong>. We repeat this process across all time steps <strong>t = 0, 1, 2, …</strong>

<div className="not-prose my-6 flex flex-wrap items-end justify-center gap-2 md:gap-3">
  {['0', '1', '2', '…'].map((t) => (
    <div key={t} className="flex flex-col items-center gap-1">
      <span className="rounded-lg border-2 border-cyan-400 bg-cyan-50 px-3 py-2 font-mono text-xs font-bold text-cyan-900 dark:border-cyan-700 dark:bg-cyan-950/40 dark:text-cyan-100">
        x<sub>{t === '…' ? 't' : t}</sub>
      </span>
      <span className="text-slate-400" aria-hidden>
        ↓
      </span>
      <span className="rounded-md border border-slate-400 bg-slate-100 px-2 py-1 text-xs font-semibold dark:border-slate-500 dark:bg-slate-800">f</span>
      <span className="text-slate-400" aria-hidden>
        ↓
      </span>
      <span className="rounded-lg border-2 border-violet-400 bg-violet-50 px-3 py-2 font-mono text-xs font-bold text-violet-900 dark:border-violet-700 dark:bg-violet-950/40 dark:text-violet-100">
        y<sub>{t === '…' ? 't' : t}</sub>
      </span>
    </div>
  ))}
</div>

While this allows us to handle <strong>sequences of inputs</strong>, each prediction <strong>y<sub>t</sub></strong> is based <strong>only</strong> on the corresponding <strong>x<sub>t</sub></strong>. There is <strong>no memory</strong> of what happened at earlier time steps. This <strong>independence assumption</strong> contradicts the very premise of sequential modeling, which relies on the idea that <em>prior context informs future states</em>.

<div className="not-prose my-5 grid gap-3 md:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-rose-300 bg-rose-50/90 p-4 dark:border-rose-800 dark:bg-rose-950/25">
    <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase text-rose-800 dark:text-rose-300">What this setup lacks</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      The ability to model <strong>dependencies across time</strong>.
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-emerald-300 bg-emerald-50/90 p-4 dark:border-emerald-800 dark:bg-emerald-950/25">
    <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase text-emerald-800 dark:text-emerald-300">Why that hurts</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      e.g. predicting a word — <em>words that came before</em> are crucial. Ignoring previous steps → poor capacity.
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

## Introducing recurrence: capturing temporal dependencies

To solve this problem, we introduce a core idea: <strong>maintain and update an internal state</strong> as the network processes each time step. Let us define this hidden or internal state as <strong>h<sub>t</sub></strong>. At each time step <strong>t</strong>, we compute:

<div className="not-prose my-5 space-y-3 rounded-xl border border-indigo-200 bg-indigo-50/40 p-4 dark:border-indigo-900/40 dark:bg-indigo-950/20">
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-indigo-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">1</span>
    <span>
      An updated internal state <strong>h<sub>t</sub></strong>, which is a <strong>function of both</strong> the current input <strong>x<sub>t</sub></strong> and the previous state <strong>h<sub>t−1</sub></strong>.
    </span>
  </div>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-indigo-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">2</span>
    <span>
      An output prediction <strong>y<sub>t</sub></strong>, which now depends on <strong>h<sub>t</sub></strong> rather than directly on <strong>x<sub>t</sub></strong> alone.
    </span>
  </div>
</div>

<strong>Formally:</strong>

$$
\mathbf{h}_t = f(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{h}_{t-1})
$$

$$
\mathbf{y}_t = g(\mathbf{h}_t)
$$

This represents how the output at time step <strong>t</strong> is computed. The output is computed using the <strong>current hidden state</strong>.

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-x-auto rounded-xl border border-slate-200 dark:border-slate-700">
  <table className="w-full min-w-[min(100%,560px)] border-collapse text-left text-sm">
    <thead>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-200 bg-slate-800 text-white dark:border-slate-600 dark:bg-slate-900">
        <th className="p-3 font-semibold">Symbol</th>
        <th className="p-3 font-semibold">Meaning</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody className="text-slate-700 dark:text-slate-300">
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-100 bg-teal-50/40 dark:border-slate-800 dark:bg-teal-950/15">
        <td className="p-3 font-mono font-bold text-teal-900 dark:text-teal-200">x<sub>t</sub></td>
        <td className="p-3">
          The input at time step <em>t</em>. For example, a word in a sentence or a stock price at time <em>t</em>.
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-100 dark:border-slate-800">
        <td className="p-3 font-mono font-bold text-teal-900 dark:text-teal-200">h<sub>t−1</sub></td>
        <td className="p-3">
          The hidden state from the previous time step (<em>t</em>−1). Think of this as the <strong>memory</strong> of the RNN.
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-100 bg-teal-50/40 dark:border-slate-800 dark:bg-teal-950/15">
        <td className="p-3 font-mono font-bold text-teal-900 dark:text-teal-200">h<sub>t</sub></td>
        <td className="p-3">
          The updated hidden state at time <em>t</em>.
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-100 dark:border-slate-800">
        <td className="p-3 font-mono font-bold text-teal-900 dark:text-teal-200">f</td>
        <td className="p-3">
          A function that combines <strong>x<sub>t</sub></strong> and <strong>h<sub>t−1</sub></strong>. This is usually a neural network layer with non-linearity (like <em>tanh</em> or <em>ReLU</em>).
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-100 bg-violet-50/40 dark:border-slate-800 dark:bg-violet-950/15">
        <td className="p-3 font-mono font-bold text-violet-900 dark:text-violet-200">y<sub>t</sub></td>
        <td className="p-3">
          The output at time <em>t</em> — computed using the <strong>current hidden state</strong>.
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="bg-white dark:bg-slate-950/30">
        <td className="p-3 font-mono font-bold text-violet-900 dark:text-violet-200">g</td>
        <td className="p-3">
          A function (often a linear layer followed by <em>softmax</em>, <em>sigmoid</em>, or <em>identity</em> depending on task).
        </td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div className="not-prose my-5 rounded-lg border border-violet-200 bg-violet-50/60 p-3 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-violet-900/40 dark:bg-violet-950/20 dark:text-slate-200">
  <p className="m-0">
    <strong>y<sub>t</sub> = g(h<sub>t</sub>)</strong> — the output at step <em>t</em> is produced from what the RNN &quot;knows&quot; at <em>t</em> (<strong>h<sub>t</sub></strong>), not from <strong>x<sub>t</sub></strong> in isolation.
  </p>
</div>

This setup provides a <strong>recurrence relation</strong>. The internal state acts as a form of <strong>memory</strong>, encoding information from past inputs and making it available to influence future computations. Crucially, this creates a <strong>dependency chain over time</strong>. The prediction at time step <strong>t</strong> indirectly depends on all inputs <strong>x₀, x₁, …, x<sub>t</sub></strong> seen so far.

This structure defines a <strong>Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)</strong>.

## Why this matters

The introduction of <strong>recurrence</strong> was a <strong>turning point</strong> in sequence modeling. It allowed neural networks to:

<div className="not-prose my-5 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-3">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-emerald-300 bg-emerald-50/90 p-4 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-emerald-800 dark:bg-emerald-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    <strong>Learn patterns</strong> that span <em>multiple time steps</em>.
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-amber-300 bg-amber-50/90 p-4 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-amber-800 dark:bg-amber-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    Maintain a form of <strong>short-term memory</strong>.
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-indigo-300 bg-indigo-50/90 p-4 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-indigo-800 dark:bg-indigo-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    <strong>Generalize</strong> across sequences of <em>different lengths</em> using a <strong>shared</strong> set of parameters.
  </div>
</div>

Although <strong>vanilla RNNs</strong> are limited in their ability to capture <strong>long-term dependencies</strong> due to issues like <em>vanishing gradients</em>, they laid the foundation for more advanced architectures such as <strong>LSTMs</strong>, <strong>GRUs</strong>, and eventually <strong>Transformers</strong>.

<div className="not-prose my-8 rounded-xl border-2 border-dashed border-indigo-400 bg-indigo-50/50 p-5 text-center dark:border-indigo-600 dark:bg-indigo-950/30">
  <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-indigo-800 dark:text-indigo-300">[Static to Time Aware]</p>
  <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-base font-semibold text-slate-900 dark:text-slate-100">
    This transition from <strong>static</strong> to <strong>dynamic</strong>, <em>time-aware</em> models marked the beginning of deep learning&apos;s foray into <strong>language</strong>, <strong>speech</strong>, and many other <em>sequence-intensive</em> domains.
  </p>
</div>
