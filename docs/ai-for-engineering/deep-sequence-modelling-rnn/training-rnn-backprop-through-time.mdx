## Extending to RNNs: The Need for Temporal Backpropagation

In the case of <strong>recurrent neural networks</strong>, we face an added challenge: <strong>temporal dependencies</strong>.

Rather than a single forward pass through a <em>static</em> network, an RNN <strong>unfolds over time</strong>. At each time step:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-200 bg-teal-50/80 p-3 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-teal-900/50 dark:bg-teal-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    It takes in a <strong>new input</strong>,
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-200 bg-teal-50/80 p-3 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-teal-900/50 dark:bg-teal-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    Updates its <strong>hidden state</strong>,
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-200 bg-teal-50/80 p-3 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-teal-900/50 dark:bg-teal-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    Produces an <strong>output</strong>,
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-200 bg-teal-50/80 p-3 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:border-teal-900/50 dark:bg-teal-950/25 dark:text-slate-200">
    And contributes to the <strong>total loss</strong>.
  </div>
</div>

This means the total loss is <strong>not</strong> just from one output, but is the <strong>sum of losses across time steps</strong>.

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathcal{L}_t
$$

To train such a model, we need to backpropagate not only through the <strong>layers</strong> of the network, but also <strong>backward through time</strong> across each time step where the hidden state was <strong>reused</strong>.

This is the motivation for a specific training algorithm:

<div className="not-prose my-5 rounded-xl border-2 border-dashed border-indigo-400 bg-indigo-50/50 p-4 text-center dark:border-indigo-600 dark:bg-indigo-950/20">
  <p className="m-0 text-base font-bold text-indigo-900 dark:text-indigo-200">Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)</p>
</div>

## Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)

<strong>Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)</strong> is the extension of backpropagation to recurrent structures. Here&apos;s what it involves:

<div className="not-prose my-6 space-y-4">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-l-4 border-indigo-500 bg-white py-3 pl-4 pr-3 shadow-sm dark:bg-slate-900/40">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      <strong>Unroll</strong> the RNN across the sequence length, treating each time step as a <em>separate layer</em> (<strong>sharing weights</strong>).
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-l-4 border-violet-500 bg-white py-3 pl-4 pr-3 shadow-sm dark:bg-slate-900/40">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Compute the <strong>total loss</strong> as the <strong>sum</strong> over all time steps (as above).
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-l-4 border-rose-500 bg-white py-3 pl-4 pr-3 shadow-sm dark:bg-slate-900/40">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      <strong>Backpropagate gradients</strong> from the <em>final</em> time step back to the <em>initial</em> one, flowing errors backward through both <strong>layers</strong> and <strong>time</strong>.
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-x-auto rounded-xl border border-slate-200 bg-slate-50/90 p-4 dark:border-slate-700 dark:bg-slate-900/50">
  <p className="m-0 mb-3 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">
    Time unrolling (conceptual)
  </p>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-center justify-center gap-2 text-center font-mono text-xs sm:text-sm">
    <span className="rounded border border-slate-300 bg-white px-2 py-1 dark:border-slate-600 dark:bg-slate-800">t = 1</span>
    <span className="text-slate-400">→</span>
    <span className="rounded border border-slate-300 bg-white px-2 py-1 dark:border-slate-600 dark:bg-slate-800">t = 2</span>
    <span className="text-slate-400">→</span>
    <span className="rounded border border-slate-300 bg-white px-2 py-1 dark:border-slate-600 dark:bg-slate-800">⋯</span>
    <span className="text-slate-400">→</span>
    <span className="rounded border-2 border-indigo-400 bg-indigo-50 px-2 py-1 font-semibold dark:border-indigo-600 dark:bg-indigo-950/40">t = T</span>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-center text-xs text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">
    Same <strong>W</strong> at every step — gradients flow <em>backward</em> through the whole chain.
  </p>
</div>

This backward flow must propagate through a chain of <strong>repeated computations</strong> — the same <strong>weight matrices</strong> and <strong>activation functions</strong> applied again and again at each step.

Gradients w.r.t. an early hidden state involve a <strong>product</strong> of many Jacobian factors along the trajectory:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_1}
\;\propto\;
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_T}
\prod_{t=2}^{T}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_t}{\partial \mathbf{h}_{t-1}}
$$

---

## Practical Challenge: Vanishing and Exploding Gradients

This <strong>repeated multiplication</strong> creates a major numerical challenge:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-4 md:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-orange-400 bg-orange-50/90 p-4 dark:border-orange-700 dark:bg-orange-950/30">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm font-bold text-orange-900 dark:text-orange-200">Exploding</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      If the derivatives at each step are <strong>slightly greater than one</strong>, the gradients can <strong>explode</strong> <em>exponentially</em>.
    </p>
    <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-center text-xs font-mono text-orange-800 dark:text-orange-300">|g| &gt; 1 → g<sup>T</sup> grows fast</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-slate-400 bg-slate-100/90 p-4 dark:border-slate-600 dark:bg-slate-800/60">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm font-bold text-slate-900 dark:text-slate-100">Vanishing</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      If they&apos;re <strong>less than one</strong>, the gradients can <strong>vanish</strong> — shrinking toward <strong>zero</strong>.
    </p>
    <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-center text-xs font-mono text-slate-700 dark:text-slate-300">|g| &lt; 1 → g<sup>T</sup> → 0</p>
  </div>
</div>

This problem is particularly damaging in sequence modeling because it prevents the model from learning <strong>long-term dependencies</strong>. If gradients either <strong>blow up</strong> or <strong>vanish</strong>, the model can&apos;t effectively use <strong>early sequence information</strong> to make <strong>late predictions</strong>.

---

## A Solution: Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)

To address this, the deep learning community proposed <strong>enhanced RNN architectures</strong> — ones that modify the <strong>recurrent unit itself</strong> to better regulate the flow of <strong>information</strong> and <strong>gradients</strong>.

The most well-known of these is the <strong>Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)</strong> network.

### What LSTMs Do Differently

<strong>LSTMs</strong> introduce <strong>gates</strong> — <em>learned mechanisms</em> that selectively control what information to <strong>keep</strong>, <strong>forget</strong>, and <strong>expose</strong> at each time step. This makes them far more robust to the <strong>vanishing and exploding gradient</strong> problems.

By introducing <strong>explicit memory cells</strong> and <strong>gating functions</strong>, LSTMs can:

<PythonPlayground minLines={22} starter={`import numpy as np

# Minimal Elman RNN trained with BPTT — predicts next bit in a repeating sequence
np.random.seed(7)
seq = [1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0]   # input sequence (t=0..6 → predict t=1..7)

hidden_size = 4
Wxh = np.random.randn(hidden_size, 1) * 0.3
Whh = np.random.randn(hidden_size, hidden_size) * 0.3
Why = np.random.randn(1, hidden_size) * 0.3
bh  = np.zeros((hidden_size, 1))
by  = np.zeros((1, 1))
lr  = 0.05

tanh   = np.tanh
sig    = lambda z: 1 / (1 + np.exp(-z))

for epoch in range(201):
    h = np.zeros((hidden_size, 1))
    total_loss = 0
    grads = {k: np.zeros_like(v) for k, v in
             zip(['Wxh','Whh','Why','bh','by'],[Wxh,Whh,Why,bh,by])}
    hs, ys, zhs = [], [], []

    # Forward through time
    for t in range(len(seq) - 1):
        x = np.array([[seq[t]]], dtype=float)
        zh = Wxh @ x + Whh @ h + bh
        h  = tanh(zh)
        y  = sig(Why @ h + by)
        hs.append(h.copy()); ys.append(y); zhs.append(zh)
        total_loss += (y - seq[t+1]) ** 2

    # BPTT
    dh_next = np.zeros_like(h)
    for t in reversed(range(len(seq) - 1)):
        x   = np.array([[seq[t]]], dtype=float)
        dy  = (ys[t] - seq[t+1]) * ys[t] * (1 - ys[t])
        grads['Why'] += dy @ hs[t].T
        grads['by']  += dy
        dh   = Why.T @ dy + dh_next
        dzh  = dh * (1 - hs[t] ** 2)
        grads['Wxh'] += dzh @ x.T
        grads['Whh'] += dzh @ (hs[t-1].T if t > 0 else np.zeros_like(hs[0]).T)
        grads['bh']  += dzh
        dh_next = Whh.T @ dzh

    for k, v in zip(['Wxh','Whh','Why','bh','by'],[Wxh,Whh,Why,bh,by]):
        v -= lr * np.clip(grads[k], -1, 1)   # gradient clipping

    if epoch % 50 == 0:
        print(f"Epoch {epoch:3d}  loss={float(total_loss):.4f}")
`} />
