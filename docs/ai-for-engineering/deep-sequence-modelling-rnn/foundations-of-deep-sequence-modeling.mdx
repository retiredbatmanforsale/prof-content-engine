## Why sequence modeling? A motivating example

Let&apos;s begin with an <strong>intuitive motivation</strong>. Imagine a ball moving in <strong>2D space</strong>. You&apos;re asked to predict its <strong>next location</strong>.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-4 md:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-rose-300 bg-gradient-to-b from-rose-50 to-white p-5 dark:border-rose-800 dark:from-rose-950/35 dark:to-slate-900/40">
    <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-rose-800 dark:text-rose-300">Case 1</p>
    <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      You only have access to the ball&apos;s <strong>current position</strong>. Any prediction you make will essentially be a <em>guess</em> the problem is <strong>underdetermined</strong>.
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-emerald-300 bg-gradient-to-b from-emerald-50 to-white p-5 dark:border-emerald-800 dark:from-emerald-950/35 dark:to-slate-900/40">
    <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-emerald-800 dark:text-emerald-300">Case 2</p>
    <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      You&apos;re also given the ball&apos;s <strong>prior trajectory</strong> and its <strong>historical positions</strong>. With this <em>sequence of past states</em>, you can now model its motion and <strong>reasonably estimate</strong> its future position.
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

This illustrates the <strong>crux of sequence modeling</strong>: incorporating <strong>historical context</strong> to improve <em>predictive accuracy</em>.

## Types of sequence modeling problems

Unlike traditional <strong>classification</strong> tasks where inputs and outputs are <strong>fixed-length</strong> and often tabular, sequence modeling introduces <strong>structured</strong> and <strong>variable-length</strong> data. Let&apos;s look at some <em>canonical problem types</em>:

## Core challenge: capturing temporal dependencies

A <strong>feedforward neural network</strong> as discussed in the previous lecture assumes that all input features are <strong>independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.)</strong>. This assumption <strong>breaks down</strong> in sequential settings.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-xl border border-slate-300 bg-gradient-to-r from-rose-50/80 via-white to-emerald-50/80 p-5 dark:border-slate-600 dark:from-rose-950/20 dark:via-slate-900/40 dark:to-emerald-950/20">
  <p className="m-0 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">In sequence modeling:</p>
  <div className="mt-4 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-3">
    <div className="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 bg-white/90 p-3 text-center text-sm font-medium text-slate-800 dark:border-slate-700 dark:bg-slate-800/80 dark:text-slate-200">
      Inputs are <strong>ordered</strong>.
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 bg-white/90 p-3 text-center text-sm font-medium text-slate-800 dark:border-slate-700 dark:bg-slate-800/80 dark:text-slate-200">
      Future predictions must <strong>respect and leverage</strong> past observations.
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 bg-white/90 p-3 text-center text-sm font-medium text-slate-800 dark:border-slate-700 dark:bg-slate-800/80 dark:text-slate-200">
      The <strong>same model</strong> must be applied across time steps or positions.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

To handle this, we need architectures that are <strong>designed to process sequences</strong>, capable of learning representations where <strong>current predictions</strong> are functions of both <em>present</em> and <em>past</em> information.

**See the difference live.** A ball moves around a circle. A feedforward predictor that sees only the current point can't tell which way it's headed; a time-aware predictor with hidden state tracks the trajectory:
