# Aletheia: when AI stops guessing and starts *proving*

:::info Primary source

This deep dive aligns with Google DeepMind’s write-up on **Gemini Deep Think** and the **Aletheia** math research agent: [Accelerating Mathematical and Scientific Discovery with Gemini Deep Think](https://deepmind.google/blog/accelerating-mathematical-and-scientific-discovery-with-gemini-deep-think/) (Thang Luong &amp; Vahab Mirrokni, February 2026).

:::

The pursuit of <strong>Artificial General Intelligence</strong> has long been haunted by a single, intractable wall: the inability of neural networks to sustain <strong>rigorous, multi-step logical consistency</strong> over the long horizons required for <em>original scientific discovery</em>. While we have seen models pass the Bar Exam or generate elegant code, the <strong>&quot;Evaluation Gap&quot;</strong>—the chasm between solving known textbook problems and uncovering novel truths—remained wide. The mathematical community has traditionally viewed Large Language Models as <strong>&quot;stochastic parrots,&quot;</strong> capable of mimicry but incapable of the deep, non-linear reasoning needed to push the frontier of human knowledge.

But the release of <strong>Aletheia</strong>, a specialized math research agent developed by <strong>Google DeepMind</strong> and powered by <strong>Gemini Deep Think</strong>, has fundamentally changed the calculus of what is possible. We are no longer talking about AI solving high school geometry; we are talking about a system that <strong>autonomously generates publication-grade research papers</strong>, resolves open conjectures from the <strong>Erdős database</strong>, and bridges the gap between competition-style puzzles and professional PhD-level inquiry. This is not just an incremental update to a model; it is the debut of a <strong>new scaling law</strong>—one that prioritizes <strong>inference-time compute</strong> over sheer parameter count. In this deep dive, we will peel back the layers of the Aletheia architecture, examine the <strong>&quot;System 2&quot;</strong> thinking loops that drive its accuracy, and explore why this marks the beginning of the era of the <strong>AI Scientist</strong>.

## Context setup: the limits of the oracle

Before Aletheia, the state-of-the-art in mathematical AI was dominated by two paradigms: the <strong>&quot;Oracle&quot; LLM</strong> and the <strong>&quot;Symbolic Solver&quot;</strong> like AlphaProof. The Oracle model, typified by standard GPT or Gemini iterations, works on a <strong>single-shot, next-token prediction</strong> basis. This is <strong>&quot;System 1&quot;</strong> thinking—fast, intuitive, but prone to <strong>catastrophic hallucinations</strong> when the logic chain exceeds a few steps. These models frequently fabricate citations and struggle with the <strong>&quot;superficial topic understanding&quot;</strong> caused by the scarcity of research-level training data.

On the other hand, <strong>AlphaProof</strong> and <strong>AlphaGeometry</strong> represented the <strong>&quot;Symbolic&quot;</strong> camp, achieving Silver-medal performance at the International Mathematical Olympiad by combining LLMs with formal languages like <strong>Lean</strong>. However, the symbolic approach is bottlenecked by a <strong>&quot;gargantuan human scaffolding project&quot;</strong>. Every problem must be painstakingly <strong>&quot;auto-formalized&quot;</strong> into code that a compiler can understand, a process that is entirely alien to how professional mathematicians actually communicate in natural language.

The evaluation gap was therefore <strong>twofold</strong>: a lack of reliability in natural language and a lack of flexibility in formal systems. Mathematicians do not work in code; they work in high-level abstractions, leveraging a vast literature that spans thousands of papers. To solve this, DeepMind realized they needed an agent that could navigate the <strong>semantic richness</strong> of natural language while maintaining the <strong>rigorous self-critique</strong> of a formal prover. This realization led to the birth of <strong>Aletheia</strong>—named after the Greek goddess of truth—designed to move beyond competition-level puzzles and into the realm of <strong>professional, open-ended research</strong>.

## Core concept: the Aletheia architecture

Aletheia is <strong>not</strong> a single static model; it is a <strong>multi-agent system</strong> designed to manage cognitive load and verify its own reasoning chains in a recursive loop. It operates on the principle that <strong>verification is easier than generation</strong>, and that <strong>iterative revision</strong> is the key to minimizing hallucinations in complex domains.

### Layer 1 — the intuition of System 2 (inference-time scaling)

The core intuition behind Aletheia is the implementation of <strong>&quot;Inference-Time Scaling&quot;</strong>. While traditional models are fixed once training ends, Aletheia allows the system to <strong>&quot;think harder&quot;</strong> by allocating more compute <em>at the moment of the query</em>. This is analogous to a human mathematician spending weeks on a single proof, exploring dozens of dead ends before arriving at a robust path. The system moves away from the <strong>&quot;one-shot&quot;</strong> response and toward a <strong>&quot;multi-path&quot;</strong> exploration strategy where the model explicitly models its internal reasoning chains.

Informally, answer quality $A$ depends on inference-time compute $C_{\text{inference}}$, model scale $N$, and external <strong>tools</strong>—not on $N$ alone. The relationship is captured in one place below (there is no single closed form for $f$; see also the <strong>non-monotonic</strong> caveat later):

$$
A \;=\; f\bigl(C_{\text{inference}},\, N,\, \text{tools}\bigr)
\quad\text{with}\quad
\frac{\partial A}{\partial C_{\text{inference}}} > 0
\;\text{ over useful ranges.}
$$

### Layer 2 — the agentic loop (Generator, Verifier, Reviser)

The architecture is composed of <strong>three primary sub-agents</strong>, each specializing in a different stage of the mathematical process. These agents interact continuously until a solution is found that passes the Verifier’s standard, or a preset computational limit is reached.

If the Verifier marks a solution as <strong>&quot;critically flawed,&quot;</strong> the Generator is triggered to start an entirely new path. If the solution is <strong>&quot;technically correct but needs fixes,&quot;</strong> the Reviser steps in to refine the specific lemmas. This <strong>decoupling of generation and verification</strong> is the primary mechanism that suppresses the <strong>&quot;superficial reasoning&quot;</strong> prevalent in earlier models.

### Layer 3 — breakthroughs in natural language reasoning

Aletheia introduces three breakthroughs that differentiate it from the AlphaProof lineage. First, it operates <strong>entirely in natural language</strong>, avoiding the friction of formal language translation. Second, it integrates <strong>&quot;Intensive Tool Use,&quot;</strong> specifically leveraging <strong>Google Search</strong> and web browsing to navigate the complexities of mathematical literature. This capability is crucial for scientific research, as it prevents the model from <strong>&quot;hallucinating citations&quot;</strong> by grounding its claims in actual published works. Third, it utilizes an advanced version of <strong>Gemini Deep Think</strong> that generates <strong>multiple chains of reasoning in parallel</strong>, allowing it to combine the best insights from different paths into a final, unified output.

## Evidence and breakthroughs: from IMO to Erdős

The performance data for Aletheia suggests that the <strong>&quot;Evaluation Gap&quot;</strong> is closing rapidly. The system has demonstrated success across a spectrum of difficulty, from high-school competitions to PhD-level research and unsolved conjectures.

### Benchmark performance and scaling

On the <strong>IMO-ProofBench Advanced</strong> test—a benchmark specifically designed to be more challenging than standard competition sets—Aletheia reached an unprecedented <strong>95.1% accuracy</strong>. This is a massive jump from the <strong>65.7%</strong> achieved by the <strong>&quot;IMO Gold&quot;</strong> version of Gemini Deep Think released just months prior.

### The Erdős conjectures and FirstProof success

The most impressive evidence of Aletheia&apos;s capability is its performance on the <strong>Bloom&apos;s Erdős Conjectures database</strong>—a collection of nearly 1,200 problems proposed by Paul Erdős. Aletheia autonomously proposed solutions to <strong>212</strong> of the remaining <strong>700</strong> unsolved problems. Human experts confirmed that while many were incorrect, <strong>13</strong> were technically correct, and <strong>4</strong> of these were entirely novel solutions to previously open questions.

Furthermore, in the <strong>&quot;FirstProof Challenge&quot;</strong>—a benchmark of 10 unpublished research-level problems—Aletheia solved <strong>6 out of 10</strong> problems with <strong>zero human intervention</strong>. Problem 7, in particular, was an <strong>&quot;atypical problem&quot;</strong> that had remained unsolved for years; Aletheia&apos;s solution was so robust that it was personally verified and lauded by the mathematician who originally proposed the conjecture.

## Insight: the paradigm shift in AI research

The success of Aletheia points toward a deeper paradigm shift in the pursuit of AGI. We are moving from the <strong>&quot;Oracle&quot;</strong> era to the <strong>&quot;Agentic&quot;</strong> era, where the primary value of an AI is not its knowledge base, but its ability to <strong>manage its own reasoning budget</strong>.

### The death of the one-shot oracle

The traditional LLM interface—a single box where you type a question and get a single answer—is inherently limited by the <strong>&quot;Single-System&quot;</strong> architecture. Aletheia proves that for high-stakes reasoning, we must <strong>decouple the &quot;Thinking&quot; from the &quot;Output&quot;</strong>. This means that the next generation of AI development will not just be about larger datasets, but about more efficient <strong>&quot;Thinking Traces&quot;</strong> and better <strong>&quot;Verifier&quot;</strong> models.

### &quot;Subconscious plagiarism&quot; and the new frontier

A profound insight from the Aletheia line of work is the risk of <strong>&quot;Subconscious Plagiarism&quot;</strong>. Because these models have ingested millions of pages of mathematical literature during pretraining, they may reproduce known proofs without realizing they are not <strong>&quot;original&quot;</strong>. This forces the mathematical community to redefine what <strong>&quot;discovery&quot;</strong> means in the age of AI. Is an AI &quot;discovering&quot; a solution if it synthesizes two unrelated papers to solve a third problem? Aletheia&apos;s ability to calculate <strong>&quot;eigenweights&quot;</strong> in arithmetic geometry using techniques from algebraic combinatorics unfamiliar to human experts suggests that <strong>&quot;Discovery&quot;</strong> in the AI era may look more like <strong>&quot;Superhuman Synthesis&quot;</strong>.

## Limitations and open questions: the overthinking penalty

Aletheia is not infallible. Its deployment on the Erdős problems revealed that while it can solve world-class questions, it still has a high <strong>&quot;False Positive&quot;</strong> rate in certain unsupervised contexts—out of <strong>212</strong> proposed solutions, <strong>137</strong> were ultimately incorrect.

### Non-monotonic scaling of thought

Research into inference-time scaling has identified a <strong>&quot;Non-monotonic trend&quot;</strong>: beyond a certain point, models can <strong>&quot;overthink&quot;</strong>—circular logic, doubting good steps—so quality need not rise forever with more compute.

$$
\exists\, C_1 < C_2 \quad \text{such that} \quad A(C_1) > A(C_2).
$$

## Creator&apos;s POV: why most people are missing the &quot;inference gap&quot;

This is the most critical take: most people are focused on the <strong>&quot;Parameter War,&quot;</strong> but the real revolution is the <strong>&quot;Inference War.&quot;</strong> Aletheia represents a move away from the idea that a model <strong>&quot;knows&quot;</strong> an answer. Instead, the model <strong>&quot;searches&quot;</strong> for an answer.

This is the beginning of the end for static benchmarks. If a model can spend <strong>100× more compute at test-time</strong> to solve a problem, then traditional <strong>one-shot</strong> evaluations like MMLU become incomplete as sole arbiters. We are entering an era where <strong>compute-per-token</strong> at inference is central to the <strong>effective scaling law</strong>. Most analysts are missing that Aletheia isn&apos;t just a better Gemini; it&apos;s a <strong>new way to use compute</strong>. The real winner in the AI race won&apos;t be the lab with the most data alone, but the lab with the best <strong>&quot;System 2&quot; architecture</strong>—the one that knows how to make its model <strong>&quot;think&quot;</strong> before it <strong>&quot;talks&quot;</strong>.
