## The limits of fully connected networks in vision

We have explored **fully connected neural networks** models where **each neuron in one layer is connected to every neuron in the previous layer**. This structure works well for problems where the input is **one-dimensional** or where **SPATIAL LOCALITY** is *not* important.

But **images are fundamentally different**.

An image is a **two-dimensional structure** — width and height (and possibly **channels** for color). Every pixel has **SPATIAL meaning**: a pixel next to another shares **more visual relevance** than one on the *opposite side* of the image. **Flattening** this structure into a **one-dimensional vector** *destroys this locality*.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-4 md:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-slate-200 bg-slate-50 p-4 dark:border-slate-600 dark:bg-slate-900/50">
    <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">Where FC layers fit</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Inputs where <strong>ordering</strong> matters but <em>neighbourhood on a grid</em> does not — or 1D signals treated as a vector.
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-rose-300 bg-rose-50/90 p-4 dark:border-rose-900/45 dark:bg-rose-950/25">
    <p className="m-0 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wide text-rose-800 dark:text-rose-300">Why images push back</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      <strong>2D layout</strong> + <strong>channels</strong> → neighbours carry signal; flattening throws that away.
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

Let&apos;s say we flatten a **100×100** image (grayscale). That&apos;s a vector of size **10,000**. If our hidden layer has **1,000** neurons, we now have **10 million parameters** just between two layers. *Worse*, we&apos;ve lost all of the **rich structure** that defines images — **edges**, **shapes**, **orientation**, and **locality**.

## What we need instead

To model vision tasks effectively, we need a network architecture that:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-teal-300 bg-teal-50/90 p-4 dark:border-teal-800 dark:bg-teal-950/30">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm font-bold text-teal-900 dark:text-teal-200">Preserves layout</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Preserves the <strong>SPATIAL arrangement</strong> of pixels.
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-cyan-300 bg-cyan-50/90 p-4 dark:border-cyan-800 dark:bg-cyan-950/30">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm font-bold text-cyan-900 dark:text-cyan-200">Local connectivity</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Maintains <strong>LOCAL CONNECTIVITY</strong> (pixels that are <em>near</em> each other matter most).
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-violet-300 bg-violet-50/90 p-4 dark:border-violet-800 dark:bg-violet-950/30">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm font-bold text-violet-900 dark:text-violet-200">Parameter efficiency</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Is <strong>parameter-efficient</strong>, sharing computations across regions of the image. <em>(downsample parameters)</em>
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-emerald-300 bg-emerald-50/90 p-4 dark:border-emerald-800 dark:bg-emerald-950/30">
    <p className="m-0 text-sm font-bold text-emerald-900 dark:text-emerald-200">Hierarchy</p>
    <p className="mt-2 mb-0 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Builds <strong>hierarchical features</strong>, where local patterns form edges, shapes, and entire objects. <em>(deeper hierarchical features)</em>
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

This is the idea behind **Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)**.

## Local connectivity and weight sharing

Instead of connecting **every input pixel** to **every neuron** in the next layer, CNNs use **local receptive fields** (**KERNELS**) in **small patches** of the input image.

Let&apos;s say we use a **filter** (also called a **kernel**) of size **4×4**. This filter is a small set of weights (**just 16 numbers**) that will be applied across the image:

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-xl border border-slate-300 bg-gradient-to-br from-slate-50 to-teal-50/40 p-5 dark:border-slate-600 dark:from-slate-900 dark:to-teal-950/20">
  <p className="m-0 text-center text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wide text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">Sliding the same 4×4 filter</p>
  <div className="mt-4 flex flex-col items-center gap-3 text-sm text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
    <div className="flex flex-wrap items-center justify-center gap-2">
      <span className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-500 bg-white px-3 py-2 font-mono text-xs shadow-sm dark:bg-slate-800">Top-left 4×4 patch</span>
      <span className="text-teal-600 dark:text-teal-400">→</span>
      <span className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-600 bg-teal-100 px-3 py-2 text-xs font-semibold dark:border-teal-500 dark:bg-teal-950/50">1st output neuron</span>
    </div>
    <span className="text-slate-400" aria-hidden>
      ↓ shift 1 pixel right
    </span>
    <div className="flex flex-wrap items-center justify-center gap-2">
      <span className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-500 bg-white px-3 py-2 font-mono text-xs shadow-sm dark:bg-slate-800">Next 4×4 patch</span>
      <span className="text-teal-600 dark:text-teal-400">→</span>
      <span className="rounded-lg border-2 border-teal-600 bg-teal-100 px-3 py-2 text-xs font-semibold dark:border-teal-500 dark:bg-teal-950/50">2nd output neuron</span>
    </div>
    <p className="mb-0 mt-1 text-center text-xs text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">
      <em>Same weights</em> everywhere — that&apos;s <strong>weight sharing</strong>.
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

The **first output neuron** in the next layer is computed by applying this filter to the **top-left 4×4 patch** of the image.

The **next neuron** is computed by applying the **same filter** to the **next 4×4 patch**, shifted **one pixel** to the right.

This continues **across the entire image**.

This operation is called a **convolution**.

## Why convolutions work so well

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-x-auto rounded-xl border border-indigo-200 dark:border-indigo-900/40">
  <table className="w-full min-w-[min(100%,720px)] border-collapse text-left text-sm">
    <thead>
      <tr className="border-b border-indigo-200 bg-indigo-600 text-white dark:border-indigo-800 dark:bg-indigo-800">
        <th className="p-3 font-semibold">Property</th>
        <th className="p-3 font-semibold">What it gives you</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody className="text-slate-700 dark:text-slate-300">
      <tr className="border-b border-indigo-100 bg-indigo-50/40 dark:border-slate-800 dark:bg-indigo-950/15">
        <td className="p-3 font-bold text-indigo-900 dark:text-indigo-200">Locality</td>
        <td className="p-3">
          Each neuron sees only a <strong>small patch</strong> of the input (<em>local features</em>).
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-indigo-100 dark:border-slate-800">
        <td className="p-3 font-bold text-indigo-900 dark:text-indigo-200">Weight sharing</td>
        <td className="p-3">
          The <strong>same filter</strong> is used everywhere in the image, which <strong>drastically reduces parameters</strong>.
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="bg-white dark:bg-slate-950/30">
        <td className="p-3 font-bold text-indigo-900 dark:text-indigo-200">Translation invariance</td>
        <td className="p-3">
          Because the same filter is applied across locations, the network can <strong>detect a pattern regardless of where it occurs</strong>.
        </td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

This is **fundamentally different** from fully connected layers, where **each neuron** has a **separate weight** for **every input pixel**.

## What is a convolution?

A **convolution** is a **sliding**, **element-wise multiplication** operation followed by a **sum**, **bias** addition, and **nonlinearity**.

Here&apos;s how it works:

<div className="not-prose my-6 flex flex-col gap-2 rounded-xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50/50 p-4 dark:border-sky-800 dark:bg-sky-950/25">
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 border-b border-sky-200 pb-2 text-sm dark:border-sky-800">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-sky-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">1</span>
    <span className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Take a small patch of the input image — say, a <strong>4×4</strong> region.
    </span>
  </div>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 border-b border-sky-200 pb-2 text-sm dark:border-sky-800">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-sky-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">2</span>
    <span className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Multiply it <strong>element-wise</strong> with the <strong>4×4</strong> filter.
    </span>
  </div>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 border-b border-sky-200 pb-2 text-sm dark:border-sky-800">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-sky-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">3</span>
    <span className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      <strong>Sum</strong> the result into a <strong>single number</strong>.
    </span>
  </div>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 border-b border-sky-200 pb-2 text-sm dark:border-sky-800">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-sky-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">4</span>
    <span className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Add a <strong>bias</strong> term.
    </span>
  </div>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 border-b border-sky-200 pb-2 text-sm dark:border-sky-800">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-sky-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">5</span>
    <span className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      Pass the result through a <strong>nonlinearity</strong> (like <em>ReLU</em>).
    </span>
  </div>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-start gap-2 text-sm">
    <span className="flex h-7 w-7 shrink-0 items-center justify-center rounded-full bg-sky-600 text-xs font-bold text-white">6</span>
    <span className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      <strong>Shift</strong> the filter (by a <strong>stride</strong> of 1 or 2 pixels), and <em>repeat</em>.
    </span>
  </div>
</div>

The result is a new matrix, called a **feature map**, which encodes **where** and **how strongly** a certain pattern appears in the image.

By **stacking multiple filters**, we extract **multiple types of features** — *edges in different directions*, *textures*, *corners*, etc.

This aligns **perfectly** with how humans understand vision — as a **composition** of *increasingly complex structures*.

:::tip[Voila moments to remember]

- **Flattening** images for fully connected layers **destroys spatial information** and is **inefficient**.
- **Convolutional layers** maintain **spatial structure** and **reduce parameters** by using **local filters**.
- A **convolution** is a **filter sliding** across the image, performing **local pattern detection**.
- **CNNs** exploit properties of images — **locality**, **translation invariance**, and **shared structure** — to build **robust visual models**.

:::
