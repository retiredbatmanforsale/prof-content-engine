Over the last decade, **deep learning** turned computer vision from **hand-tuned features and brittle rules** into **models that learn straight from pixels**. That change is not a small tweak; it is a different way to think about **perception**.

| | **Earlier mainstream approach** | **Deep learning–centric approach** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Features** | Handcrafted (edges, corners, histograms, heuristics) | Learned layers that build their own representations |
| **Pipeline** | Many separate stages, lots of manual design | Often **end-to-end**: pixels (or tensors) in, predictions out |
| **Where it shines** | Constrained, stable setups | Large data, varied scenes, many industries |

Neural networks are now the **default engine** for serious vision work—from **datacenter training** down to **phones and wearables**.

## Vision in healthcare and biology

Deep learning is used where images already carry life-critical signal: **find the abnormality**, **classify tissue**, **triage workload**.

| **Setting** | **What vision models often do** |
| --- | --- |
| **Radiology / CT / MRI** | Flag suspicious regions, rank cases for urgent read |
| **Pathology slides** | Assist tissue and cell-level patterns |
| **Retina (e.g. diabetic retinopathy)** | Screening support for eye specialists |

They can surface **subtle, repeatable cues** across many cases—always as **tools next to clinicians**, not a wholesale replacement of judgment.

---

## Self-driving: a hard test for vision

Driving stacks **perception**, **prediction**, and **planning** under **safety pressure**. One famous research direction (including work at **MIT**) asked: can a model map **camera pixels → driving commands** **end-to-end**, without a classic chain of hand-designed perception + map + planner modules?

| | **Idea in one line** |
| --- | --- |
| **Input** | Raw video from forward cameras |
| **Output** | Steering / drive commands |
| **Appeal** | One network; can **generalize to new roads** without a handcrafted HD map in the loop |
| **Reality check** | Elegant, but **very hard**: rules, rare events, and safety need more than a single loss on pixels |

So this story is less “problem solved” and more “**why industrial autonomy still mixes learned vision with systems engineering**."

## Facial recognition: from “face or not” to rich signals

| **Phase** | **Typical goal** |
| --- | --- |
| **Classical / early CV** | **Detect** a face: is there a face in the image? |
| **Modern deep learning** | **Detect** + **align** + **embed**: landmarks, pose, identity under occlusion, gaze, expressions, attention |

Same camera stream; **much richer outputs**—and stricter needs for **privacy**, **bias**, and **consent** in real products.

## Takeaway

Deep learning did not just **improve** accuracy charts; it made **one flexible toolkit** for vision across **cloud, edge, medicine, mobility, and interfaces**. The rest of this track builds the **core ideas** behind those models; the next lessons dig into **how** they work—not only **where** they ship.

## Try it: zero-shot vision with CLIP

The "one flexible toolkit" claim above is easy to wave away in prose. Here it is concretely. CLIP is a vision-language model trained on image-caption pairs scraped from the web. It learned a *shared embedding space*: an image and a sentence describing that image end up close together; an unrelated sentence ends up far away.

Once you have that, **classification stops being a fixed list of categories**. You feed the model an image and *whatever candidate captions you want* — `"a photo of a cat"`, `"an indoor scene"`, `"the year 1985"` — and it scores how well each one matches. No retraining, no fine-tuning, no labeled dataset. The same model handles "is this a cat?" and "is this happy?" because it lives in language-space.

Pick a preset image (or upload your own), tweak the candidate labels, and watch the scores shift:
