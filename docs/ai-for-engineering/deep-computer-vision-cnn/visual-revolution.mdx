# Lesson 1: The Visual Revolution

:::tip[Start here: vision & CNNs]

**Computer vision** is machine learning on **images or video**: you take a grid of pixels (often with extra sensors or metadata) and produce **outputs a product can use**—class labels, bounding boxes, segmentation masks, depth maps, poses, tracks, or signals for control. In production, that almost always means coping with **noise, lighting, motion blur, occlusion, and data that doesn’t match your training set**. Your job as an engineer is to design and train models that stay **correct and robust enough** when the world refuses to look like the benchmark.

:::

## Introduction to vision as a human and machine capability

For humans, sight is one of the main ways we understand the world—reading faces, streets, and traffic lights, often **without thinking hard about each step**. This lesson is about the same idea for **machines**: using **raw images or video** so computers can **see** (in a useful, engineered sense). Deep learning is the usual tool today for turning pixels into predictions.

## What is vision, conceptually?

A compact definition: **vision is figuring out what is where, from what you see.** One image still forces you to settle many questions at once:

- **What** objects or regions matter?
- **Where** are they, and how big are they?
- Are things **moving** or still?
- How do parts of the scene **relate** to each other?
- What might **happen next**?

So perception is not only “paint the right label on each pixel.” It blends **space** (layout), **time** (motion and change), and **prediction** (what the scene suggests will happen).

## Static vs. dynamic perception

We don’t only recognize **things**; we read **situations**. Same street, different story:

- A **parked van** reads as static; a **moving sedan** reads as motion—even if both are “cars.”
- **Pedestrians on the sidewalk** vs. **stepping into the road** change how you interpret risk and intent.
- A **red light** isn’t just a color; it cues **how traffic should behave next.**

**Takeaway:** useful vision often needs **how the world changes over time**, not a single frozen frame. Building systems with that in mind is a big part of modern computer vision.

## Why computer vision matters

You’ll see the same pattern in many industries: **sense the scene → decide or act**, often under **latency** pressure.

- **Driving & mobility** — lanes, people, signs, obstacles  
- **Healthcare** — findings in scans and images  
- **AR & robotics** — maps, poses, object and scene understanding  
- **Manufacturing** — scratches, dents, assembly mistakes  

These problems usually need more than a single **class label**; they need **where**, **what**, **how it’s changing**, and sometimes **what to do next**—often in **real time**.
