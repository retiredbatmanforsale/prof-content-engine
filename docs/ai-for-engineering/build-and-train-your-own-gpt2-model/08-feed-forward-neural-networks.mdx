## Where we are in the build

After Lesson 7 your `Block` is `x + attn(LN(x))` — a residual-wrapped, pre-normalised attention layer. It can mix information **across tokens**: position $t$ now sees a weighted blend of all earlier positions' values. What it cannot do is meaningfully *transform* those values within a single token.

That's because attention's "transformation" is a learned linear projection ($W_O$), and the convex-combination-then-project pipeline is essentially still linear. Stack twelve linear-ish layers and you get a slightly-non-linear linear-ish function — not enough to express the actual computation language modelling needs (lookup tables, conditional logic, n-gram-style patterns, factual recall).

The fix is the **feed-forward network** — a small two-layer MLP applied independently at every token position, with a non-linearity in between. Same MLP shared across all positions; same MLP shared across all tokens in the batch. Sometimes called "position-wise FFN" because it's pointwise in the time dimension.

Today's lesson finishes `blocks.py`. After this, your `Block` is the full GPT-2 block — exactly what gets stacked twelve times in GPT-2 small.

## The naive solution — skip the FFN

Try a transformer where every block is `x + attn(LN(x))` and nothing else. Twelve attention layers, twelve LayerNorms, no MLPs. What does it learn?

Empirically: not much. Such a model trains, slowly, but it caps out at much higher loss than a full transformer with the same parameter count redistributed elsewhere. It can do soft routing of token information, but it can't apply rich per-token transformations. You can't easily learn "this token is a verb in past tense" because the transformation from token vector to "verb-in-past-tense vector" is non-linear, and attention alone doesn't do non-linear computation.

A more practical demonstration: take the FFN's hidden dimension to zero and watch validation loss climb. Or, equivalently, replace the MLP with the identity. Either way, you've lost the per-token computation tier. The model collapses to something like a deep linear model with attention mixing — far less expressive than language requires.

Why is one non-linearity not enough? Stacks of linear functions are linear. Even with attention mixing, the per-position transform $x \mapsto W_O \cdot \text{convex\_comb}(W_V x_1, \ldots, W_V x_T)$ is linear in the $x$'s. **Without an explicit non-linearity, the model has no way to learn anything that isn't a sum of linear projections of past tokens.** The MLP injects the non-linearity that makes the whole stack a universal function approximator.

## The motivated solution — per-token MLP

The original [Transformer paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) settled on a now-canonical FFN:

$$
\text{MLP}(x) \;=\; W_2 \cdot \phi\!\left(W_1 x\right)
$$

with $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{4C \times C}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times 4C}$, and $\phi$ a non-linear activation. Three design choices:

### Why $4 \times C$ for the hidden dimension?

The hidden layer is **wider** than the input/output dimension by a factor of 4. This isn't deeply principled — Vaswani et al. picked it empirically and it stuck. The intuition: a wider hidden layer gives the network room to project onto many feature directions, apply non-linearities, and recombine. A 1× hidden would be too tight (linear bottleneck); 8× would be wasteful. 4× has been the default for nearly every transformer since 2017, including GPT-2, GPT-3, and Llama.

The cost: this layer dominates the parameter budget. For GPT-2 small ($C = 768$):

$$
\text{params per MLP} \;=\; \underbrace{C \cdot 4C}_{W_1} \;+\; \underbrace{4C \cdot C}_{W_2} \;=\; 8 C^2 \;=\; 8 \cdot 768^2 \approx 4.72 \text{M}
$$

Across 12 blocks that's ~56.6M parameters — about **45% of GPT-2 small's 124M**. The MLP is the largest single contributor.

### Why GELU and not ReLU?

[GELU](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.08415) (Gaussian Error Linear Unit) was introduced by Hendrycks and Gimpel in 2016 and adopted by GPT-2:

$$
\text{GELU}(x) \;=\; x \cdot \Phi(x)
$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. Roughly: GELU is a smooth, probabilistic version of ReLU. It "decides" how much of $x$ to pass through based on $x$'s value, rather than the hard 0/1 gate that ReLU uses.

Practical reasons GPT-2 prefers GELU over ReLU:

- **Smooth gradient near zero.** ReLU has a kink at 0 — gradient jumps from 0 to 1. GELU is differentiable everywhere; the gradient varies smoothly with $x$. Better-behaved optimisation, especially with adaptive optimisers like Adam.
- **Allows small negative values through.** ReLU zeros out negative inputs, killing the gradient for them ("dying ReLU"). GELU lets a small fraction of negative inputs through, keeping their gradient alive. Empirically: a few tenths of a percent better validation perplexity at GPT-2 scale, for the same training compute.

GPT-2 specifically uses the **tanh approximation** to GELU:

$$
\text{GELU}_{\text{approx}}(x) \;=\; 0.5 x \!\left(1 + \tanh\!\left(\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}(x + 0.044715 x^3)\right)\right)
$$

…because the exact form involves the $\Phi$ CDF, which is slow to compute. The approximation is within $10^{-4}$ of the exact value across the relevant input range and computes ~3× faster. PyTorch's `nn.GELU(approximate="tanh")` uses this form. nanoGPT and the original OpenAI GPT-2 codebase do too.

### Code: extend `blocks.py`

```python
# nanogpt-from-scratch/blocks.py  (Lesson 8 — full GPT-2 block)

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

from attention import MultiHeadAttention
from config import GPTConfig


class MLP(nn.Module):
    """Position-wise feed-forward: Linear(C, 4C) -> GELU -> Linear(4C, C) -> Dropout."""

    def __init__(self, config: GPTConfig):
        super().__init__()
        self.fc = nn.Linear(config.n_embd, 4 * config.n_embd, bias=False)
        self.act = nn.GELU(approximate="tanh")
        self.proj = nn.Linear(4 * config.n_embd, config.n_embd, bias=False)
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(config.dropout)

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.dropout(self.proj(self.act(self.fc(x))))


class Block(nn.Module):
    """Full GPT-2 transformer block. Pre-LN, attention + residual, MLP + residual."""

    def __init__(self, config: GPTConfig):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln1 = nn.LayerNorm(config.n_embd)
        self.attn = MultiHeadAttention(config)
        self.ln2 = nn.LayerNorm(config.n_embd)
        self.mlp = MLP(config)

    def forward(self, x):
        x = x + self.attn(self.ln1(x))
        x = x + self.mlp(self.ln2(x))
        return x
```

Three things worth flagging:

- **Position-wise.** `nn.Linear` on a `(B, T, C)` tensor implicitly applies the same `(C → 4C)` transformation independently at every (batch, time) position. There is no "communication" between tokens inside the MLP — that's attention's job, and we've already done it before reaching the MLP.
- **`bias=False`.** Same reason as in attention: GPT-2 turns biases off everywhere they're not strictly required. They cost parameters without measurably improving performance at scale.
- **Two residual sums per block.** `x + attn(...)` mixes across tokens; `x + mlp(...)` transforms within each token. The block has *two* gradient highways now, not one.

## Math by typing it

The full transformer block, pre-LN, in one line of math:

$$
\text{Block}(x) \;=\; x' + \text{MLP}(\text{LN}_2(x'))
\quad \text{where} \quad
x' \;=\; x + \text{Attn}(\text{LN}_1(x))
$$

The MLP itself:

$$
\text{MLP}(x) \;=\; W_2 \cdot \text{GELU}(W_1 x)
$$

GELU exact form:

$$
\text{GELU}(x) \;=\; x \cdot \Phi(x) \;=\; \tfrac{x}{2}\!\left(1 + \text{erf}\!\left(\tfrac{x}{\sqrt 2}\right)\right)
$$

GELU tanh approximation (used by GPT-2, nanoGPT, this course):

$$
\text{GELU}_{\text{approx}}(x) \;=\; 0.5 x \!\left(1 + \tanh\!\left(\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}(x + 0.044715 x^3)\right)\right)
$$

Parameter accounting per Block, using GPT-2 small ($C = 768$):

| Component                 | Parameters       | Fraction of block       |
|---------------------------|------------------|--------------------------|
| `ln1` ($2C$)              | 1,536            | 0.02%                   |
| `attn` ($4C^2$)           | 2,359,296        | 33.3%                   |
| `ln2` ($2C$)              | 1,536            | 0.02%                   |
| `mlp` ($8C^2$)            | 4,718,592        | 66.6%                   |
| **Block total**           | **7,080,960**    | 100%                    |

The MLP is **twice the size of the attention** by parameter count. Across 12 blocks, ~56.6M of GPT-2 small's 124M parameters live in the MLP layers. This is sometimes called the "key-value memory" of the model — a body of work [(Geva et al. 2021)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.14913) argues that the MLP layers store the bulk of factual knowledge, with attention acting as the routing mechanism that decides which facts to recall.

```python
import torch

from config import GPTConfig
from blocks import Block

config = GPTConfig()
block = Block(config)

# Shape sanity.
B, T, C = 2, 16, config.n_embd
x = torch.randn(B, T, C)
y = block(x)
assert y.shape == x.shape, "Block must preserve (B, T, C)"

# Parameter count breakdown.
def count(module):
    return sum(p.numel() for p in module.parameters())

print(f"ln1:  {count(block.ln1):>10,}")
print(f"attn: {count(block.attn):>10,}    (= 4 * C^2 = {4 * C**2:,})")
print(f"ln2:  {count(block.ln2):>10,}")
print(f"mlp:  {count(block.mlp):>10,}    (= 8 * C^2 = {8 * C**2:,})")
print(f"--- block total: {count(block):,} ---")

# Stack 12 of them like GPT-2 small.
stack = torch.nn.Sequential(*[Block(config) for _ in range(12)])
print(f"\n12-block stack: {count(stack):,} params")
# Should be roughly 12 * 7,080,960 = 84,971,520
# Plus embeddings (Lessons 2-3) = ~84.9M + 39.4M = ~124.4M ≈ GPT-2 small.
```

Twelve blocks plus the embeddings from Lessons 2–3 should land at approximately 124M parameters — within rounding error of GPT-2 small's published count. We're one final LayerNorm and one output projection away from the real thing. Lesson 10 wires those in.

## What you can now do

- Build the canonical position-wise FFN: `Linear(C, 4C) → GELU → Linear(4C, C)`.
- Justify the 4× hidden expansion, GPT-2's choice of GELU (and its tanh approximation), and the per-token-wise structure.
- Account for ~66% of every transformer block's parameters and ~45% of GPT-2 small's total — the MLP is where the model spends its capacity.
- Recognise the full GPT-2 block: pre-LN, attention + residual, pre-LN, MLP + residual. **Two LNs, two residuals, two sub-layers.** This is what gets stacked.
