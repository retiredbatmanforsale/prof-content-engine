Deep transformers stack **many** attention and FFN blocks. Without **normalization**, activations can **drift** in scale layer after layer—gradients become **noisy** and training **unstable**. **Layer normalization (LayerNorm)** fixes a **simple** statistic per **token**: it recenters and rescales that token’s **feature vector**, then applies a **learnable** gain and bias so the model can **undo** normalization where needed.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-300 bg-gradient-to-br from-violet-50 via-white to-indigo-50 p-5 shadow-sm dark:border-violet-800/60 dark:from-violet-950/40 dark:via-slate-950 dark:to-indigo-950/40">
  <p className="m-0 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-widest text-violet-700 dark:text-violet-300">Core formula</p>
  <div className="mt-4 overflow-x-auto text-center">
    <span className="font-mono text-base text-slate-900 dark:text-slate-100 sm:text-lg">LayerNorm(x) = γ · (x − μ) / √(σ² + ε) + β</span>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-3 mb-0 text-center text-xs text-slate-600 dark:text-slate-400">Same structure as in [Vaswani et al. (2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) <strong>Add &amp; Norm</strong> blocks · walkthrough on [Lex AI — Attention is All You Need](https://aiayn.aiseekhegaindia.com/)</p>

## Which axis gets normalized?

In a transformer, a **token** at one position is a vector **x** ∈ ℝᵈᵐᵒᵈᵉˡ (one row of shape **(batch, seq_len, d_model)**).

**LayerNorm** computes **μ** and **σ²** across the **d_model** **features** of **that row only**—**not** across other tokens in the sentence and **not** across the batch (by default in the classic formulation).

## The mathematics (step by step)

For a **single** token vector **x** with **d** components **x₁ … x_d**:

$$
\mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_{j=1}^{d} x_j, \qquad
\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{d}\sum_{j=1}^{d}(x_j - \mu)^2
$$

Then (with **ε** tiny for numerical safety):

$$
\hat{x}_j = \frac{x_j - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \varepsilon}}, \qquad
\text{LN}(x)_j = \gamma_j \,\hat{x}_j + \beta_j
$$

**γ** (gamma) and **β** (beta) are **learnable vectors** of length **d**—**element-wise** scale and shift so the network can **recover** expressive power after forcing **unit-scale** statistics.

Compact **vector** form (as in the paper and libraries):

$$
\text{LN}(x) = \gamma \odot \frac{x - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \varepsilon}} + \beta
$$

where **⊙** is **element-wise** multiplication and **μ, σ²** are **broadcast** scalars across **d** dims.

## LayerNorm vs BatchNorm (why transformers use LayerNorm)

    <thead>
      <tr className="bg-slate-700 text-white dark:bg-slate-900">
        <th className="p-3 font-semibold">BatchNorm (typical CNN)</th>
        <th className="p-3 font-semibold">LayerNorm (Transformer)</th>

    </thead>
    <tbody className="text-slate-800 dark:text-slate-200">
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-200 dark:border-slate-700">

        <td className="p-3">Statistics over **feature dim** for **each** token row</td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-200 dark:border-slate-700">

        <td className="p-3">**No** dependence on batch size for the norm axis</td>
      </tr>
      <tr className="border-b border-slate-200 dark:border-slate-700">
        <td className="p-3">Designed for **fixed** grid shapes</td>
        <td className="p-3">Natural for **variable seq lengths** (with padding masks elsewhere)</td>
      </tr>

        <td className="p-3" colSpan={2}>

## Where LayerNorm sits: **Add &amp; Norm**

Residuals (**Lesson 8**) and LayerNorm work as a **pair**:

- **Post-norm (paper):** apply **Sublayer**, **add** residual, then **LayerNorm**.
- **Pre-norm (common):** **LayerNorm** first, then **Sublayer**, then **add**.

Either way, **norm** keeps the **residual stream** in a **stable** numeric band so **attention softmax** and **FFN** nonlinearities behave predictably at **large depth**.
