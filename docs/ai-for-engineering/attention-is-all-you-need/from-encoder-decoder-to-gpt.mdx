The 2017 paper presented a transformer for **machine translation**: encoder reads the source, decoder writes the target, cross-attention bridges the two. Within two years, the field had taken every component apart and reassembled them into three distinct families — and only one of them looks like the original. This lesson is the **map** of what happened next, and where to go to actually build one.

---

## The split

The encoder and decoder of the 2017 paper were originally inseparable: each was useless for translation without the other. But researchers quickly noticed that the **encoder alone** is a powerful representation learner, and the **decoder alone** — given causal masking — is a perfectly good language model. Once you remove the requirement that input and output be different sequences, the architecture splits in three.

## A short timeline

| Year | Model | Family | Why it mattered |
|------|-------|--------|-----------------|
| 2017 | **Transformer** (Vaswani et al.) | Encoder–decoder | The paper this course is about. Beat seq2seq+attention RNNs on translation. |
| 2018 | **GPT-1** (Radford et al.) | Decoder-only | Showed transformer LMs work as a *general* pretraining objective, not just translation. |
| 2018 | **BERT** (Devlin et al.) | Encoder-only | Masked language modelling on the encoder. Took over the NLP benchmarks for two years. |
| 2019 | **GPT-2** (Radford et al.) | Decoder-only | Scaled to 1.5B parameters. Showed convincing zero-shot generation. *This is the model the [companion course](../build-and-train-your-own-gpt2-model/intro) builds.* |
| 2019 | **T5** (Raffel et al.) | Encoder–decoder | Unified every NLP task as text-to-text. The 2017 architecture, scaled and unified. |
| 2020 | **GPT-3** | Decoder-only | 175B parameters. Few-shot learning emerged at scale. |
| 2022 | **InstructGPT / ChatGPT** | Decoder-only + RLHF | Made decoder-only LMs follow instructions and stay on-topic in conversation. |
| 2023+ | Frontier LLMs | Mostly decoder-only | The pattern won. Most production assistants today are scaled decoder-only transformers. |

---

## Why decoder-only won

By 2020 the field had a clear answer to "which family scales best?" — and the answer was **decoder-only**. Three reasons:

1. **One stream, one objective.** Next-token prediction is a single, dense, self-supervising signal you can run on any text. No paired source/target needed, no special masking schedule. You point the model at the internet and it trains.
2. **Tasks reframe as prompts.** Translation? Prompt: *"Translate to French: ___"*. Summarization? *"Summarize: ___"*. Once you accept this framing, the encoder side is no longer required — the model just continues the sequence.
3. **Scaling laws favoured it.** Empirically, decoder-only models showed cleaner loss-vs-compute curves at large parameter counts. A simpler, more uniform architecture is easier to scale on thousands of GPUs.

This does **not** mean encoder-decoder is dead. T5-style models still win on tasks where you have a clearly delineated *source* and *target* (translation, summarization with a long source, multimodal pipelines where one tower encodes audio or images). But the cultural and economic centre of gravity shifted decisively to decoder-only.

---

## What the 2017 paper still gives you

Even if you spend the rest of your career working with decoder-only models, the 2017 architecture is worth understanding because:

- **The mechanisms are the same.** Self-attention, multi-head attention, residuals, layer norm, FFN — every one of them is in GPT-2, GPT-4, Claude, and every other modern LLM.
- **Cross-attention is reappearing.** In multimodal models (Flamingo, GPT-4V, modern speech systems), cross-attention is back — one tower encodes the image or audio, another generates the response. The 2017 design is the template.
- **Encoder-only models are still load-bearing.** Every embedding-based retrieval system, every reranker, every BERT-style classifier in production today descends directly from the encoder you just learned.
