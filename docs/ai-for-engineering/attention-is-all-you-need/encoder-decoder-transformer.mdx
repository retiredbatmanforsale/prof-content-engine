# Encoder–decoder transformer — conclusion

This course ends where [Vaswani et al. (2017), *Attention Is All You Need*](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) presented the **full** model: an **encoder** that **reads** an **input** sequence in one representation, and a **decoder** that **writes** an **output** sequence while **querying** that representation through **cross-attention**. The [Lex AI interactive tour](https://aiayn.aiseekhegaindia.com/) shows the same **two-tower** picture: **source → encoder memory (K, V)** feeding **every** decoder layer.

The encoder–decoder design is **not** the only transformer layout—but it is the canonical choice when the **input** and **output** are **different** sequences and you want a **dedicated, bidirectional** encoding pass **before** autoregressive generation.

## Where is the encoder–decoder transformer used?

Typical **real-world** settings when **input** and **output** are **both** sequences and you want a **clear split** between **understanding the input** and **generating the output**:

- **Machine translation** (the paper’s headline task)
- **Abstractive summarization** (document → summary) in **seq2seq** setups
- **T5**-style **text-to-text** frameworks (treat many tasks as **input text → output text**)
- **Speech recognition** and similar pipelines where an **encoder** ingests audio frames and a **decoder** emits text
- **Multimodal** systems where one **tower** encodes images or video and a **decoder** generates captions or answers

In all of these, the **encoder** is doing **work the decoder does not repeat**: building a **fixed** (per forward pass) **memory** of the **whole** input for the decoder to **consult** at **every** step via **cross-attention**.

## Why have an **encoder** at all?

The **encoder** answers: “What does the **entire input** mean in context, **before** we commit to output words?”

- It runs **bidirectional** self-attention over the **source**—every token sees every other **source** token.
- It produces a **sequence of vectors** that become **K** and **V** for **cross-attention**. The **decoder** then issues **queries** (**Q**) that **select** and **blend** those vectors **per** generation step.

That **separation** is powerful: **one** deep pass to **compress and contextualize** the input; **many** decoder steps that **reuse** the same memory instead of re-encoding the source inside a single causal stream.

---

## Why can’t the **GPT** family “just do the same thing”?

**GPT-style** models are **decoder-only**: there is **no** second stack whose sole job is **bidirectional** encoding of a **fixed** source, and there is **no** **cross-attention** to dedicated **K, V** from that stack.

You can **simulate** some tasks by **stuffing** the source into the **prompt** and letting the causal model attend **only leftward**—that often works remarkably well—but it is **not** the same architecture:

- The model does **not** get a **full bidirectional** view of the source in a **separate** encoder; everything lives in **one** causal context window.
- **Long inputs** compete for **context length**; an **encoder** can be sized and trained for **rich input encoding** while the **decoder** focuses on **generation**.

So: **GPT** can **approximate** many **A → B** tasks **in software** (prompting, instruction tuning), but the **native** **encoder–decoder** design is built **exactly** for **input sequence → different output sequence** with **explicit** **encoder memory** and **cross-attention**.

---

## Why can’t the **encoder-only** “BERT family” do the same natively?

**BERT-style** models lack an **autoregressive decoder** stack. They excel at **understanding** or **scoring** text (classification, **MLM**, embeddings) but they are **not** trained as **standard** **seq2seq** generators that emit a **new** sequence **token by token** with **causal** self-attention and a **linear + softmax** head for **open-ended** generation.

You can **bolt on** decoders or use **task-specific** heads, but the **classic** **BERT** architecture alone does not implement the **full** **encoder–decoder** loop from the **original** Transformer paper.

---

## Significance in the real world

- **Encoder–decoder** remains the **natural** blueprint when teams want **explicit** **input encoding** + **conditional** **generation** (translation, T5-like unification, many **ASR** and **multimodal** designs).
- **Decoder-only** scaled to **massive** **general** LMs because a **single** stream simplifies **training** and **product** (chat, completion).
- **Encoder-only** dominates when you need **rich bidirectional** **representations** without generating long text.

No single variant “replaces” the others; they **optimize** for **different** **interfaces** between **input**, **representation**, and **output**.

---

## Training objective (recap)

The decoder is trained with **teacher forcing** and **cross-entropy** on **next target tokens**, conditioned on **previous** target tokens (via **masked** self-attention) and on the **encoder** (via **cross-attention**). The encoder is trained **end-to-end** with the decoder so **K, V** are useful for the **downstream** prediction task.
