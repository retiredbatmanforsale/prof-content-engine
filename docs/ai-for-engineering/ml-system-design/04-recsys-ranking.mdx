## 1. Where we are in the framework

Still inside **Step 3 of the 6-step framework** — model architecture — now applied to the **ranking** layer. Time-budget: **~8 minutes**.

By the end you should have:

- A **wide-and-deep style architecture** drawn, with feature inputs, embedding lookups, dense layers, and **multi-task heads**
- A **loss design** with a one-line justification (pointwise / pairwise / listwise)
- An **explicit calibration step** between the model output and the production score
- A **score combination rule** that fuses the multi-task heads into the final ranking score

Retrieval (Lesson 3) optimised recall over 1B items. Ranking optimises **precision** over the ~100 candidates retrieval surfaced. The shape of the model is completely different — that's the point.

---

## 2. The problem at this layer

Input: ~100 candidate videos from retrieval, plus the user, plus context. Output: an ordered list, top-10 of which goes into the slate after re-ranking.

The constraint: **the ranker can be expensive**, because it only runs over 100 items, not 1B. A 12-layer transformer with cross-features that would have been impossible in retrieval is now plausible in ranking. The latency budget for the ranker is roughly **80ms** of the total 200ms — about 8× the retrieval budget.

The metric is **predicted-watch-time NDCG** offline, **CTR / watch time / completion rate / day-7 retention** online — the multi-objective stack from Lesson 1. The ranker has to optimise the metric tree, not a single number.

## 3. The naive solution — and why it breaks

The naive answer is one or two sentences:

> "I'll train a logistic regression on hand-engineered features to predict click probability, and rank the candidates by predicted probability."

Three failures.

### 3.1 Hand-engineered features lose to learned crosses

Ranking quality depends overwhelmingly on **feature crosses** — interactions between user-side and item-side features. "User likes hip-hop" × "video is hip-hop" is more informative than either feature alone. Hand-engineering all useful crosses for 100M users and 1B items is impossible. Production rankers learn the crosses via factorisation machines or deep architectures.

### 3.2 Single objective ≠ the metric tree

A binary click model predicts click probability. The metric tree from Lesson 1 said the system optimises **watch time** primarily, with click as a calibration signal. Optimising click probability alone reproduces the L4-ceiling failure of recommending clickbait that gets clicked but unwatched.

### 3.3 Uncalibrated outputs break the rest of the system

Logistic regression trained on **biased samples** (per Lesson 2's IPW + sampled negatives) produces logits that **do not match true click probabilities**. The model can rank correctly while being miscalibrated. Why does that matter?

- **Online auctions** (e.g. ads) need predicted probabilities, not just rankings. An uncalibrated 0.5 from one model and 0.5 from another do not mean the same thing.
- **A/B test interpretation** depends on probabilities being comparable across model versions
- **Multi-task fusion** (combining `predicted_watch_time × predicted_click_rate × predicted_like_rate` into one score) is broken if any term is miscalibrated — the product term dominates incorrectly

This is the calibration trap. The model's AUC can be excellent and its **probabilities still be wrong**, because AUC is rank-invariant. Calibrate explicitly or pay the cost downstream.

## 4. The motivated solution

### 4.1 Wide-and-deep architecture

The canonical ranker shape, established by Cheng et al. (2016) for Google Play and adapted everywhere since:

- **Wide part** — a linear model over a small set of **manually crossed** features (e.g. `user_country × video_country`, `user_topic × video_topic`). Captures memorisation: "users who watched X also watched Y."
- **Deep part** — embeddings for high-cardinality categorical features (user ID, video ID, channel ID, topic ID) concatenated with dense numerical features, fed through 3–6 fully-connected layers with ReLU. Captures generalisation: "users like this *kind* of video."
- **Joint training** — a single loss back-propagates through both arms; the final logit is the sum.

A modern variant — **DLRM** (Naumov et al., 2019, Facebook) — replaces the wide part with **factorisation-machine-style explicit pairwise feature interactions** before the dense layers. Both architectures are correct interview answers; pick one and defend it.

### 4.2 Multi-task heads

A single head predicting one metric is the L4 mistake. A modern ranker has **multiple heads sharing a common backbone**:

| Head | Output | Loss | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Watch time** | $\hat{t} \in [0, T_{\max}]$ | quantile or log-MSE regression | Primary objective |
| **Click** | $\hat{p}_c \in [0, 1]$ | BCE | Calibration head + secondary signal |
| **Completion** | $\hat{p}_{\text{comp}} \in [0, 1]$ | BCE | Quality calibration |
| **Like** | $\hat{p}_l \in [0, 1]$ | BCE | Sparse explicit feedback |
| **Long-watch** (≥30s) | $\hat{p}_{\text{lw}} \in [0, 1]$ | BCE | Anti-clickbait calibration |

The heads share representations (the backbone learns user / item / context features once) but specialise on the final layer. The combined ranking score is a weighted geometric or arithmetic combination:

$$
\text{score} = \hat{t}^{\alpha} \cdot \hat{p}_c^{\beta} \cdot \hat{p}_{\text{comp}}^{\gamma} \cdot \hat{p}_l^{\delta}
$$

The exponents $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta$ are **not learned**. They are tuned via **online A/B tests** — Lesson 6 covers the experimentation that finds the right combination. This is a structural insight: the model learns predictions; the **weighting of those predictions is a product decision**, ramped via online experiments.

### 4.3 Calibration — the step most candidates skip

The model's output probabilities are wrong. They rank correctly but the absolute values are off, because:

- The training set has **non-natural negative sampling** (per Lesson 2) — popular items are over-represented in negatives
- **Class imbalance** during training shifts logits
- **Distillation** or label smoothing introduces additional miscalibration

The fix is a post-hoc calibration step on a held-out set:

- **Platt scaling** — fit a single sigmoid $\sigma(a \cdot \hat{p} + b)$ that maps raw model probabilities to calibrated ones; minimal data, parametric
- **Isotonic regression** — fit a non-decreasing step function from raw to calibrated; needs more data, non-parametric, more flexible

You measure calibration with a **reliability diagram** (predicted bin vs observed empirical rate) and the **expected calibration error**:

$$
\text{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^M \frac{|B_m|}{n} \left| \text{acc}(B_m) - \text{conf}(B_m) \right|
$$

where $B_m$ is the $m$-th bin, $\text{acc}(B_m)$ is the empirical click rate in that bin, and $\text{conf}(B_m)$ is the average predicted probability. ECE = 0 means perfect calibration.

A production ranker re-fits its calibrator **every retrain**. Calibration drifts as the population shifts (Lesson 7).

### 4.4 Loss design — pointwise vs pairwise vs listwise

Three loss families, in order of complexity:

- **Pointwise** — treat each (user, item) pair independently. Loss is BCE for binary heads, MSE / quantile for regression. Easiest to implement, easiest to train on logged data, but **loses ranking-specific signal** (it doesn't know that examples within a query are competing).
- **Pairwise** — for each (user, query) pair, sample positive and negative items and minimise a hinge or BCE-style loss on the score difference. Bradley-Terry models, RankNet. Captures relative ordering directly.
- **Listwise** — optimise a ranking metric (NDCG, MAP) directly. LambdaMART, ListNet. Hard to optimise (NDCG is non-differentiable; LambdaMART uses gradients of swap-deltas), but most aligned with the actual objective.

Most production rankers run **pointwise as the primary loss** (it scales) plus **a pairwise auxiliary** (it nudges the model toward correct relative ordering). Listwise is rare in production except in search-ranking systems.

The interview-grade answer: "Pointwise multi-task with a pairwise auxiliary; listwise is too expensive at this scale."

## 5. Back-of-envelope math

### 5.1 Ranker compute budget

Latency budget for the ranker is ~80ms. With 100 candidates and a 4-layer MLP at $d = 512$, each forward pass is roughly 1ms on a GPU — 100ms total without batching, **8ms with proper batched inference** (one batched forward over the 100 candidates). That fits.

If the architecture grows to a 12-layer transformer over user history (~50 items per user) ranking 100 candidates, you are looking at roughly 200ms per request — over budget. Production teams use this as the forcing function: distillation to a smaller student model, or a **two-stage ranker** (cheap ranker for 100→30, expensive ranker for 30→10).

### 5.2 Calibration sample size

Platt scaling fits 2 parameters; a few thousand samples is enough. Isotonic regression fits a step function; tens of thousands of samples are typical. A common production setup: re-fit the calibrator weekly on a fresh held-out slice of logged data — 50K to 500K samples — alongside the model retraining cadence (Lesson 7).

### 5.3 Multi-task fusion math

For the score combination $\text{score} = \hat{t}^\alpha \cdot \hat{p}_c^\beta$, taking logs:

$$
\log \text{score} = \alpha \log \hat{t} + \beta \log \hat{p}_c
$$

So tuning $(\alpha, \beta)$ is **linearly searchable in log-space**. A grid sweep with online A/B tests — say, 5 settings of $\alpha$ × 5 settings of $\beta$ run as a 5×5 factorial — converges in a few weeks. This is why **Lesson 6 (A/B testing) is load-bearing for this lesson** — without rigorous online tuning, the multi-task fusion is broken.

## 6. What the interviewer is actually grading

By minute 32 — the rough end of Step 3 (combined retrieval + ranking) — the interviewer is checking whether you can hold the difference between the two stages in your head.

- **Did you propose wide-and-deep, DLRM, or an equivalent named architecture?** "A neural network ranker" is L4 ceiling.
- **Did you propose multi-task heads?** Single-objective is the canonical L4 mistake.
- **Did you mention calibration unprompted?** This is the single fastest L5 promote signal in this round. Most candidates forget it.
- **Did you name a calibration method?** Platt or isotonic, with a one-liner about when to pick each.
- **Did you discuss loss design?** Pointwise / pairwise / listwise with reasoning.
- **Did you tie multi-task fusion back to A/B testing?** "We tune the combination weights online" is the L5 closing move.

Failure modes to avoid:

- **Skipping calibration.** Your AUC can be perfect and your probabilities can still be wrong. If you treat ranking as pure ordering, downstream systems break in subtle ways.
- **Single objective.** Even at L4, propose at least two heads.
- **Treating ranking as scaled-up retrieval.** They are different shapes of model. The dot-product two-tower from Lesson 3 is *deliberately* incapable of doing what the ranker does — and vice versa.
- **Forgetting the 100→10 narrowing.** Ranking outputs ~10 final items; the rest of the slate (re-ranking) is a separate set-level step covered in Lesson 4 of the framework, fused with diversity / freshness rules at production time.
