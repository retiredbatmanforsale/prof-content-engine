## 2. The problem at this layer

Recsys eval (Lesson 6) had a clean signal: clicks, watch time, retention. Users tell you, in real time, whether the recommendation worked.

RAG eval has none of that. The user reads an answer; either they like it or they don't, and even if they like it the answer might still be wrong. There is no objective "click" that maps to correctness. The system has to **measure its own quality** through proxies — and the proxies have to be trusted.

Three failure modes the eval system has to detect:

- **Retrieval miss** — the relevant passages weren't in the top-K, so generation has nothing to ground in
- **Faithfulness failure** — passages were retrieved but the model still hallucinated or cited incorrectly
- **Answer-quality failure** — the answer is faithful and grounded but doesn't actually address the user's question

A system that only measures end-to-end "answer quality" cannot distinguish these — and therefore cannot debug them. Component-wise eval is the unlock.

## 3. The naive solution — and why it breaks

The naive answer:

> "We run our RAG on a benchmark like MS MARCO or BEIR and report the score."

Four failures.

### 3.1 Benchmarks measure narrow proxies on public data

MS MARCO, BEIR, MMLU and similar benchmarks have known queries, known passages, known answers. They are useful for **comparing model architectures** and for **regression-testing during model upgrades**. They are **not useful for telling you if your RAG works on your corpus for your users.** A model that wins MS MARCO can lose on enterprise wiki search because the query distribution and passage style differ.

### 3.2 End-to-end metrics conflate failure modes

Asking "is this answer good?" gives one number. That number cannot tell you whether the system is failing because retrieval missed (a Lesson 9 problem), generation hallucinated (a Lesson 10 problem), or answer quality is just shallow (a Lesson 8 metric-tree problem). You cannot fix what you cannot localise.

### 3.3 LLM-as-judge isn't trusted out of the box

A common shortcut is "use GPT-4 to grade the answers." This works **after calibration** to human ratings — and is wildly unreliable before. Different judge models grade the same answer 1-2 points apart on a 5-point scale; they have systematic biases (preferring longer answers, preferring confident-sounding answers, preferring answers that mention their own provider's products). The judge has to be **anchored to a human gold set** and the calibration has to be measured.

### 3.4 Sampling without statistical structure

"We checked some examples and they look good" is not an eval. Production teams need a **standing held-out set** for offline regression-testing, a **rotating sampled human-eval set** for online decisions, and a **continuous ship-time eval** that scores every response as it goes out.

## 4. The motivated solution

### 4.1 Component-wise eval

Every shipped answer gets scored on three axes, independently:

- **Retrieval recall** — was the right passage in the top-K? Measured against a labelled set of (query, gold-passage) pairs from in-domain queries. Recall@5, recall@10. This isolates Lesson 9's failures.
- **Generation faithfulness** — given the retrieved passages, did the answer ground every claim? Measured by passage-level entailment (does the passage entail each claim in the answer?) plus citation accuracy (does the cited passage actually support the cited claim?). This isolates Lesson 10's failures.
- **End-to-end answer quality** — does the answer address the user's question, with appropriate scope, tone, and completeness? Measured via LLM-as-judge calibrated to humans. This isolates Lesson 8's metric-tree failures.

Tools that bundle these: **Ragas** (faithfulness + answer relevancy + context precision/recall), **TruLens** (similar primitives), **DeepEval**. The L5 candidate names at least one and reasons about its primitives, not just "we use Ragas."

### 4.2 Faithfulness via per-claim entailment

The faithfulness metric is computed in three steps:

1. **Decompose the answer into claims** — split into atomic factual statements (typically via an LLM "claim extractor" or via citation tags themselves)
2. **For each claim, score entailment** against the cited passage(s) — does the passage support the claim? Returns a probability in $[0, 1]$ from a small NLI model or a structured LLM judge
3. **Aggregate** — mean entailment across claims, with strict-mode (any claim below threshold = fail) for high-stakes domains

The metric:

$$
\text{faithfulness} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \text{entailment}(\text{claim}_i, \text{passages}_{\text{cited}_i})
$$

A faithfulness of 1.0 means every claim is fully entailed. Production targets are typically 0.9+ for general search and 0.99+ for high-stakes domains (medical, legal, financial). The gap between 0.9 and 0.99 is **two orders of magnitude of work**, but it is what the high-stakes products require.

### 4.3 Citation accuracy as a separate measurable

Citation accuracy answers a different question from faithfulness: when the answer says "[doc-3] supports this claim," does it? Two failure modes:

- **Wrong citation** — the cited passage doesn't actually support the claim
- **Missing citation** — a factual claim has no citation tag at all

Both are detectable post-generation with a fast NLI step. Production teams treat citation accuracy as a **guardrail**: any response below threshold is logged, sometimes withheld and replaced with a refusal.

### 4.4 LLM-as-judge with human calibration

End-to-end answer quality is graded by an LLM-judge prompted with the query, the answer, and (optionally) the retrieved passages. Naively running this gives unreliable scores. The discipline that makes it reliable:

1. **Build a human gold set** — ~300-500 examples rated by humans on a 5-point scale, with at least 2 raters per example to measure inter-rater reliability
2. **Run the LLM-judge** on the same gold set
3. **Compute calibration** — Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ between LLM scores and human-mean scores, plus mean absolute error
4. **Iterate the judge prompt** until calibration meets a threshold ($\rho > 0.8$ is typical for production-grade)
5. **Use the calibrated judge** at scale, with periodic re-calibration (the judge drifts as the underlying model is updated, and the corpus drifts over time)

The L5 candidate names $\rho$ as the calibration target. The L4 candidate uses LLM-as-judge with no calibration and trusts the output.

### 4.5 Hallucination detection on every shipped answer

Beyond batch eval, every response shipped to a user passes through a **passage-attribution scorer** in real time:

- Decompose the response into claims (same step as faithfulness)
- For each claim, run a fast entailment check against the retrieved passages
- If any claim falls below a confidence threshold, **either** withhold the response and replace with a refusal, **or** annotate the unsupported claims with a warning badge

This adds ~50-100ms to the response path (a small NLI model per claim) but catches the residual hallucinations that the prompt-level grounding from Lesson 10 missed. It is the last line of defence between the model and the user.

### 4.6 Online human eval at scale

For online decision-making (shipping a new model, comparing prompts), automated metrics are necessary but not sufficient. A standing **rotating human-eval set** of ~100 representative queries per week, rated by humans on the 5-point scale, gives the team statistical confidence to ship.

The math: with $\sigma \approx 1.0$ on a 5-point scale, detecting a 0.2-point lift at 80% power requires roughly:

$$
n = \frac{16 \sigma^2}{\delta^2} = \frac{16 \times 1}{0.04} = 400 \text{ samples per arm}
$$

## 5. Back-of-envelope math

### 5.1 Faithfulness measured at scale

For a system shipping 1M responses/day at a target faithfulness of 0.95, you expect ~50K responses/day with at least one unsupported claim. If you run claim-level entailment at 50ms per claim and an average of 5 claims per response:

$$
1 \times 10^6 \text{ responses} \times 5 \text{ claims} \times 50\text{ms} = 2.5 \times 10^5 \text{ s of compute / day}
$$

That is roughly **3 GPU-days per day** of NLI compute — non-trivial but tractable. For high-stakes domains where you can't afford a 5% unsupported-claim rate, this cost is the price of trust.

### 5.2 LLM-judge calibration cost

Building a 500-example human gold set at ~$3/example (15 min of an annotator's time at $12/hr) is $1,500 — a one-time cost. Running the judge against the gold set during prompt iteration (~10 prompt iterations, ~500 LLM-judge calls each, ~2K tokens per call):

$$
10 \times 500 \times 2000 \times \frac{\$1.5}{10^6} = \$15
$$

Calibration is **cheap**. Skipping it because "the LLM-judge is good enough" is one of the most common L4 mistakes.

### 5.3 Holdout for regression testing

A standing held-out set of 1,000 queries with gold answers is the regression test that runs on every model or prompt change. Each run costs ~1,000 × $0.002 = $2 in inference (per Lesson 10's cost) plus ~$10 in faithfulness scoring. Running it on every PR is essentially free relative to the team-time it saves.

## 6. What the interviewer is actually grading

By minute 40 of the round (Step 4 wrapping for RAG) the interviewer is checking whether you can defend the system's quality with measurement, not vibes.

- **Did you decompose eval?** Retrieval recall, faithfulness, answer quality — three components, three metrics.
- **Did you propose passage-level entailment for faithfulness?** Or did you reduce faithfulness to "answer quality"?
- **Did you mention citation accuracy as separate?** Even one sentence is L5.
- **Did you calibrate the LLM-judge?** Naming Spearman correlation against a human gold set is the L5 closing move on this round.
- **Did you propose hallucination detection on every shipped answer?** This separates demos from production.
- **Did you compute human-eval sample size?** 400-per-arm for 0.2-pt MDE is a memorable number.
- **Did you flag where benchmarks help and where they mislead?** Shows production maturity.

Failure modes to avoid:

- **Single number eval.** Component-wise or nothing.
- **Trusting LLM-judge without calibration.** Calibrate first, then trust.
- **No hallucination detection.** "We trust the model" is L3.
- **Treating benchmarks as proof.** They benchmark; they don't prove.
