## 1. Where we are in the framework

This is **Step 1 of the 6-step framework** — define the problem — applied to a different problem class. Time-budget: **~8 minutes**.

The framework hasn't changed. The metric tree shifts (faithfulness becomes primary, not watch time). The unit shifts (a cited answer, not a slate of items). The funnel shifts (LLM generation replaces ranking). Everything else — the discipline of metric layering, the demand for a defended unit, the requirement to pre-commit to a funnel before talking model — is identical to Lesson 1.

By the end of this step you should have:

- A **layered metric stack** with **faithfulness/groundedness as the primary metric**
- A **unit of recommendation** — a single answer composed of generated text plus citations to retrieved passages
- A **multi-stage RAG funnel** drawn on the whiteboard
- A one-line cost intuition explaining why RAG is **roughly 100× more expensive per request** than the recsys ranker from Lesson 4

---

## 2. The problem at this layer

The interviewer hands you the prompt:

> **"Design a search system that answers natural-language queries with citations."**

This is the modern surface — the round being asked at OpenAI, Anthropic, Perplexity, Glean, customer-support tooling, legal research, internal enterprise search, and increasingly at FAANG-class generalists running their own LLM-backed search products in 2026.

Clarification questions you ask out loud:

- **What is the corpus?** Open web (~10B documents)? Enterprise wiki (~1M)? Product docs (~10K)? Domain shapes everything downstream.
- **What is the user expecting?** A direct answer with citations? A summary of relevant docs? A list of links? They are different products.
- **What latency budget?** Real-time chat (~1-3s end-to-end) is different from offline batch summarisation (minutes are fine).
- **What freshness?** Today's news? Yesterday's? Never changes? Index strategy depends on this.
- **What domain?** General-purpose? Medical / legal / financial (high-stakes hallucination cost)? Domain shapes both the model and the eval.

Pick **a general-purpose enterprise search product** for the rest of the lesson — corpus of ~10M chunks, latency budget ~2s, freshness within hours, mostly text. This is the modal interview prompt.

## 3. The naive solution — and why it breaks

The naive answer:

> "I'll send the user's query to GPT-4 and return whatever it generates."

This fails in **four distinct ways**, and the strong interviewer will probe at least three.

### 3.1 Hallucinations without grounding

A bare LLM produces text that *sounds* authoritative whether or not it is correct. Asked "What was Acme Corp's Q3 2025 revenue?" the model returns a confident number that may or may not be real. The system is **fluent and unfaithful**. For most production search use cases — enterprise, legal, medical, news — this is unshippable.

### 3.2 No access to fresh or proprietary data

LLM training cutoffs lag months to years. Internal company docs were never in the training set. A bare LLM cannot answer "What does our refund policy say about a 90-day window?" with confidence — and will hallucinate a plausible-sounding policy if asked.

### 3.3 No citations, no traceability

Even when the bare LLM happens to produce correct facts, the user has **no way to verify**. The model cannot point to the document the claim came from because it did not look at any document. Citation is structurally impossible without retrieval.

### 3.4 No guardrail metric

The naive design optimises for fluency — does the answer read well? — but cannot detect the failure modes above. An L4 candidate proposes "use a benchmark to measure quality." But generic benchmarks (MMLU, MS MARCO) measure narrow proxies on public data, not the actual product question of "is this answer faithful and useful for *my* corpus?"

## 4. The motivated solution

The production answer has three components: a metric tree with faithfulness on top, a unit definition that includes citations, and a three-stage funnel.

### 4.1 The metric tree — faithfulness is primary

For a production RAG system the metric stack inverts the recsys ordering. Fluency / answer quality is **secondary** to faithfulness:

- **Business goal:** users get useful, trustworthy answers to real-world questions
- **Product metric:** answer-acceptance rate (thumbs up / down), citation-click rate (signal that citations are real), time-to-resolution for support, weekly active users
- **ML objective stack (in priority order):**
  1. **Faithfulness / groundedness** — every claim in the answer is supported by a retrieved passage. Operationally measured as passage-level entailment or citation accuracy. **Primary metric.**
  2. **Answer quality** — does the answer address the question, with appropriate scope and tone. Measured via LLM-as-judge calibrated to human ratings (Lesson 11 covers this).
  3. **Citation accuracy** — when the answer cites passage [doc-N], does that passage actually support the claim. Often a guardrail, not a primary.
  4. **Latency** — time to first token, total response time. Constraint, not objective.
  5. **Cost** — tokens-in × tokens-out × price. Constraint.

Saying this in the interview, out loud:

> *"Faithfulness is the primary metric — every claim has to be supported by a retrieved passage. Answer quality and citation accuracy are guardrails. Latency and cost are constraints. A fluent but unfaithful answer is a regression, not a win."*

That sentence alone is an L5 promote signal in this round.

### 4.2 The unit — a cited answer, not a ranked list

The unit is **one answer**, composed of:

- A natural-language response (200–800 tokens for most products)
- **Inline citations** — `[doc-3], [doc-7]` — pointing into the retrieved passage set
- A **bibliography section** at the bottom listing the cited passages with source links

This is **categorically different** from the recsys unit (an ordered slate of items). The retrieval and reranking layers serve the **generation step**, not the user directly. The user sees the answer; the passages are an artefact the system needs to ground its claims.

Why this matters:

- **Citation enforcement** is a structural requirement of the system, baked into the prompt and the eval — not an optional add-on
- **Refusal is a feature**, not a bug. If the retrieved passages don't support a confident answer, the system should say so explicitly
- **Cost scales with output length**, not with number of items. A long answer is expensive even if the user only reads the first sentence

### 4.3 The three-stage RAG funnel

You cannot stuff every relevant document into the LLM context window and you wouldn't want to even if you could (cost, distraction, latency). The canonical resolution is a **three-stage funnel**:

<RAGFunnel />

The four layers, top to bottom:

| Layer | Input | Output | What runs here |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Corpus** | All docs | All docs | Indexed once, refreshed on doc updates |
| **Retrieval** | Query, all docs (~10M chunks) | Top ~50 candidates | Bi-encoder (dense + sparse hybrid) + ANN — Lesson 9 |
| **Reranking** | ~50 candidates | Top K = 5–10 passages | Cross-encoder reranker — Lesson 9 |
| **Generation** | Top-K passages + query + system prompt | Cited answer | LLM with prompt-enforced citation — Lesson 10 |

Each stage trades different things. **Retrieval is recall-oriented** (don't drop the right answer; cheap). **Reranking is precision-oriented** (every passage in context costs tokens; expensive cross-encoder is worth it). **Generation is composition-oriented** (compose a faithful answer; the most expensive stage by far).

A bare LLM (the naive answer) compresses all three stages into a single forward pass. The funnel **gives the system the structure to be both fast and faithful** at scale.

## 5. Back-of-envelope numbers (cost intuition)

A quick numeric anchor to show you reason in real numbers. The headline: **RAG is roughly 100× more expensive per request than the recsys ranker from Lesson 4.**

A typical RAG request burns:

- **Retrieval:** ~5ms p99 against an indexed corpus (Lesson 9 covers the math); negligible cost vs the LLM stage
- **Reranking:** cross-encoder over 50 candidates at ~2ms each = ~100ms; small dollar cost
- **LLM generation input tokens:** 5 passages × ~512 tokens = ~2,500 tokens of context, plus ~500 tokens for the system prompt and the user query — total ~3,000 input tokens
- **LLM generation output tokens:** ~500 tokens for a typical answer

At a representative price point of **$0.50 per 1M input tokens** and **$1.50 per 1M output tokens** (mid-2026 prices for a strong open-weight model served on an H100):

$$
\text{cost per request} \approx \frac{3000 \times 0.5}{10^6} + \frac{500 \times 1.5}{10^6} = 0.0015 + 0.00075 \approx \$0.002
$$

Two-tenths of a cent per request. Sounds cheap. At 100K daily active users running 10 queries/day:

$$
10^5 \times 10 \times \$0.002 = \$2{,}000 \text{ per day} \approx \$730{,}000 \text{ per year}
$$

This is a **real budget item** in a way that recsys serving cost almost never is. By comparison, the recsys ranker from Lesson 4 — a 4-layer MLP over 100 candidates — costs fractions of a millisecond and effectively no marginal dollars per request. **The cost shift drives the system design**: you cannot afford to call the LLM speculatively, you must batch and stream aggressively, and answer length is an actual cost lever.

## 6. What the interviewer is actually grading

By minute 8 of the 45-minute round — the rough end of Step 1 for this prompt — the interviewer has formed a strong prior. For RAG specifically, the grading dimensions are:

- **Did you ask clarification questions about the corpus?** "Is this the open web or an enterprise corpus?" is the first L5 question to volunteer.
- **Did you put faithfulness on top of the metric tree?** Putting answer quality first is the L4 mistake.
- **Did you define the unit as a cited answer, not a ranked list?** A surprising number of candidates copy recsys here and lose the round structurally.
- **Did you draw a three-stage funnel?** "Use a vector DB" without retrieval + rerank + generation as separate stages is L4 ceiling.
- **Did you mention cost?** RAG cost per request is unique — the L5 candidate calls it out within the first eight minutes.
- **Did you mention refusal as a feature?** Acknowledging "if the passages don't support an answer, the system says so" is an L5 detail that signals product maturity.

Failure modes to avoid:

- **Treating RAG as recsys with words.** It is not. The unit is composed, not ranked. Citations are structural, not decorative.
- **Optimising for fluency.** Fluent + unfaithful is the canonical failure of bare-LLM systems. Faithfulness must be primary.
- **Skipping the reranker.** Going from retrieval directly to generation forces the LLM to filter noise out of context. Cross-encoder reranking is cheap insurance.
- **Forgetting cost.** Every other ML round can hand-wave cost. RAG cannot — it dominates the operational story.
