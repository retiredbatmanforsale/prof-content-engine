## 2. The problem at this layer

A model deployed today is wrong by next month. Three independent forces drive the decay.

### 2.1 Three kinds of drift

- **Feature drift** — input distributions change. Users move countries, devices change, traffic mix shifts. The features the model trained on no longer have the same distribution at serving time.
- **Label drift** — the marginal distribution of the target changes. Click rates rise during the holidays; watch time falls during exam season; new content categories take off and old ones decline.
- **Concept drift** — the relationship $P(y \mid x)$ between features and labels changes. The same user with the same features clicks on different things this year. This is the hardest drift because it can hide behind stable feature distributions.

A model can have **stable features and stable labels and still decay** because of concept drift. A monitoring system that only watches input distributions misses this.

### 2.2 The recsys feedback loop

Recsys is **uniquely afflicted** by a feedback loop that other ML systems don't have:

- The model recommends items
- Users watch the recommended items (and not the items the model didn't surface)
- Engagement on recommended items becomes the **next training set's labels**
- The next model is trained on data that is biased by the **previous** model's choices

This means the system **converges to its own assumptions**. Items the previous model down-ranked never get a chance to show they were good — their training-data signal stays low. Popular items get more popular. Niche content collapses. The catalogue effectively shrinks.

Naming this — and proposing **exploration mechanisms** to break it — is the single fastest L5 promote signal in this round.

### 2.3 Ground-truth latency

You don't know if a recommendation was good for **days or weeks**. Day-7 retention is exactly what it says — measured 7 days later. By the time the metric arrives, the model has been training on it for 7 days against the wrong signal. This is structurally unavoidable; it shapes everything about the monitoring stack.

## 3. The naive solution — and why it breaks

The naive answer:

> "We retrain the model weekly on the latest data."

Three failures.

### 3.1 One-tier retraining is wrong for every tier

Different parts of the system decay at different speeds. **Item embeddings** for fresh content need to update within minutes — yesterday's upload is invisible without it. **Ranker weights** can be retrained daily, balancing freshness against compute cost. The **two-tower retrieval model** can be retrained weekly because user-side feature distributions move slowly. A single weekly cadence is too slow for embeddings and too fast (read: expensive) for retrieval. **A multi-tier cadence is required.**

### 3.2 No drift detection

"Retrain weekly" assumes the calendar is the right trigger. Sometimes the calendar is — sometimes it isn't. A sudden shift (a viral trend, a regional event, a creator policy change) requires retraining **now**, not next Tuesday. Drift detectors automate this.

### 3.3 No deployment safety net

Pushing a freshly trained model to 100% of traffic with no validation is the canonical "shipped on Friday, paged on Saturday" story. Production needs **shadow** (model runs in parallel, predictions logged but not served) and **canary** (gradual ramp with auto-rollback) layers between training and full deployment.

## 4. The motivated solution

### 4.1 Multi-tier retraining cadence

| Tier | Cadence | What retrains | Why this cadence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Real-time** | minutes | Item embeddings (cold-start for new uploads); incremental embedding updates | Fresh content has zero engagement signal until it surfaces; latency-of-discovery is a product metric |
| **Daily** | overnight | Ranker model (Lesson 4) | Engagement signals shift daily; calibration must be re-fit per Lesson 4 |
| **Weekly** | full rebuild | Two-tower retrieval model + full feature pipeline + calibration recalibration | Catalogue shifts and user-side distribution changes; expensive but rare |

Each tier costs a different amount and buys different freshness. The L5 candidate names all three with reasoning, not just one.

### 4.2 Drift detectors with the Population Stability Index

The standard drift metric is **PSI** — a symmetric divergence between the training distribution $p$ and the serving distribution $q$, computed per feature:

$$
\text{PSI} = \sum_i (q_i - p_i) \ln \frac{q_i}{p_i}
$$

Rules of thumb:

| PSI | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| $< 0.1$ | No meaningful drift |
| $0.1 - 0.25$ | Moderate drift; investigate |
| $> 0.25$ | Significant drift; retrain |

PSI is computed per feature, daily, against the training distribution. When any critical feature crosses 0.25, an **auto-retrain trigger** fires. This means retraining is **event-driven**, not just calendar-driven.

For the **label distribution**, the equivalent check is on $P(y)$ over recent days vs the training-set $P(y)$. For **concept drift**, you need a held-out scoring set that you re-score periodically and compare to logged outcomes.

### 4.3 Shadow + canary + auto-rollback

The deployment pipeline:

1. **Shadow** — the new model receives every request the production model receives, but its predictions are **logged, not served**. This validates online performance without exposing users. A shadow run typically lasts 1-3 days.
2. **Canary** — predictions are served to a small fraction of users (1%), with the **same A/B testing apparatus** from Lesson 6 watching primary and guardrail metrics in real time.
3. **Ramp** — if guardrails hold, ramp to 10% → 50% → 100% over days.
4. **Auto-rollback** — at any stage, if a guardrail metric (retention, latency, error rate) drops below a pre-set threshold, **traffic is automatically reverted** to the previous production model within minutes.

The auto-rollback is the most important sentence on the whiteboard. Without it, the team is the rollback path — and the team takes hours, not minutes.

### 4.4 Breaking the feedback loop

Two production-grade mitigations beyond just monitoring:

- **Exploration injection** — randomly insert a small fraction of items the model would not have ranked high, to gather counterfactual signal. Costs short-term metrics; pays back in long-term diversity and freshness.
- **Off-policy correction** — when training, use IPW (Lesson 2) and counterfactual logging (Lesson 6) to weight examples by the inverse of their selection probability under the previous policy. The model trains as if it had seen a more diverse data distribution than it actually did.

These are L5+ moves. Naming the feedback loop is the gate; proposing one of these mitigations is the promote signal.

## 5. Back-of-envelope math

### 5.1 PSI worked example

A categorical feature `device_type` has training distribution: mobile 70%, desktop 25%, TV 5%. Serving today: mobile 60%, desktop 25%, TV 15%. Compute PSI:

$$
\text{PSI} = (0.6 - 0.7) \ln \tfrac{0.6}{0.7} + (0.25 - 0.25) \ln \tfrac{0.25}{0.25} + (0.15 - 0.05) \ln \tfrac{0.15}{0.05}
$$

$$
= (-0.1)(-0.154) + 0 + (0.1)(1.099) = 0.0154 + 0 + 0.1099 \approx 0.125
$$

This is in the moderate-drift band — investigate but don't auto-retrain yet. If TV grows further (say to 25%), PSI would jump past 0.25 and the auto-trigger fires.

### 5.2 Expected lifetime of a stale model

If model NDCG decays at roughly **0.2% relative per day** (a typical rate for a busy recsys), then after 30 days the model is **6% worse** than at deployment. After 90 days, ~15% worse. The break-even retraining cadence — where the cost of retraining equals the cost of decay — is typically **between 1 and 7 days** for the ranker, which is why daily retraining is the production default.

### 5.3 Canary detection power

A canary at 1% traffic over 1 day might collect ~10K samples per arm. The MDE (per Lesson 6's power formula) is roughly:

$$
\delta = \sqrt{\frac{16 \sigma^2}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{16 \times 60^2}{10{,}000}} \approx 2.4 \text{ minutes}
$$

A 6% regression on watch time (~2.4 minutes from a 40-minute baseline) is detectable in a day at 1% canary. Smaller regressions need either longer canary or a larger fraction. This is why the ramp is gradual: small regressions need more samples to detect.

## 6. What the interviewer is actually grading

By minute 53 — Step 5 wrapping and Step 6 (the brief 5-minute summary) starting — the interviewer is closing the round. They are checking whether you can land the system into production responsibly.

- **Did you name three kinds of drift?** Feature, label, concept — naming all three is L5.
- **Did you mention the feedback loop?** This is recsys-specific; flagging it unprompted is the single fastest L5 signal in this round.
- **Did you propose multi-tier retraining?** Real-time / daily / weekly with reasoning beats one-line "weekly."
- **Did you name PSI or KL?** Drift detection without a metric is L4 ceiling.
- **Did you describe shadow + canary + auto-rollback?** "Shipping safely" is what L5 candidates own; L4 candidates assume someone else owns it.
- **Did you tie back to A/B test guardrails?** Auto-rollback uses Lesson 6's guardrails as its trigger — the system is one connected story.

Failure modes to avoid:

- **One-line "we'll retrain it."** Multi-tier or nothing.
- **Forgetting the feedback loop.** Recsys monitoring without it is incomplete.
- **No deployment safety net.** "Push to 100% and hope" is the L3 answer; you should be past it.
- **No ground-truth latency awareness.** Day-7 retention is measured 7 days late — the monitoring stack has to operate with that lag.
