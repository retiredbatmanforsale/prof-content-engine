## 1. The prompt

> **"Design Google's ad ranking system."**

Same shape of prompt as Flagship 1's "Design YouTube recommendations" — but the answer differs in three structural ways. The interviewer is testing whether you spot them.

---

## 2. The wrinkles — what's different from Flagship 1

### 2.1 Auction mechanics

Ad ranking is not pure machine learning ranking. The serving system runs a **second-price auction** at request time. The ranking score is **not** just predicted relevance — it is

$$
\text{score} = \text{pCTR} \times \text{bid} \times \text{quality\_factor}
$$

The winner pays the **second-highest score**'s price (adjusted by ratios) — a generalised second-price (GSP) auction or its modern descendants (VCG, position-based). This means the ranking decision is downstream of an economic mechanism, not just a model output.

### 2.2 Calibration is mandatory, not optional

In Lesson 4, we said calibration matters for organic ranking; uncalibrated probabilities break A/B-test interpretation. **For ads, miscalibration directly destroys revenue.** If your model says `pCTR = 0.10` and the true rate is `0.05`, you have over-bid by 2× — winning auctions you would have lost at the right price, paying more than the impression is worth, and burning advertiser trust. The L4 ceiling on this round is reached the moment a candidate forgets calibration.

### 2.3 Counterfactual logging is forced

The recsys feedback loop (Lesson 7) is uncomfortable; the ads feedback loop is operationally lethal. The auction outcome depends on **which ads were shown** — and which ads were shown depends on the ranker. To estimate revenue under a new ranker without shipping it, you need **propensity logs**: for every shown impression, log the probability that the impression would have been served under the current policy. With propensities you can run inverse-propensity-weighted off-policy evaluation. Without them, your A/B test is the only signal — and at the cost-per-impression of ads, that is too slow and too expensive to be the only tool.

## 3. Walking the framework

### 3.1 Define the problem

The metric tree shifts toward economic outcomes:

- **Business goal** — revenue per query, advertiser satisfaction (renewal rate, CPL ceiling), user satisfaction (low ad-load tolerance, organic content preserved)
- **Product metric** — RPM (revenue per mille impressions), CTR, post-click conversion rate, ad-quality scores from user surveys, advertiser quality score
- **ML objective stack** — calibrated **pCTR** as primary; calibrated **pConversion** as secondary; ad-quality scoring (relevance + landing page experience) as a guardrail

The unit is **a slot of N ad impressions** (typically 1–4) embedded in the search results page. The funnel mirrors Lesson 1's recsys funnel — corpus of eligible ads, retrieval, ranking, auction — but with the **auction at the bottom** as the slot-allocation step.

### 3.2 Data pipeline

Inherits the data pipeline from Lesson 2 (clicks are biased, position bias, training-serving skew, feature store, temporal split). The ad-specific additions:

- **Conversion labels lag** — clicks are observed in seconds, conversions in hours-to-days. Multi-task heads training on clicks and conversions need different label horizons.
- **Advertiser-side features** — bid, budget remaining, frequency capping state, advertiser historical quality. These are first-class features, not optional.
- **Counterfactual propensity** — at serving time, log not just the chosen ad but the propensity (or sampling probability) under the current policy.

### 3.3 Model architecture

Inherits the wide-and-deep / DLRM ranker pattern from Lesson 4. Ad-specific additions:

- **Multi-task heads** for pCTR, pConversion, ad-quality classification, and (for some networks) post-click revenue regression
- **Calibration head** explicitly tuned on a held-out auction trace, not just a held-out prediction set, because the auction shifts the natural class distribution
- For retrieval (the pre-auction shortlist of eligible ads), the same two-tower / ANN architecture from Lesson 3 applies — gated by advertiser eligibility (budget, geography, device, daypart)

### 3.4 Train and evaluate

Loss design and calibration follow Lesson 4. The ad-specific evaluations:

- **Offline: counterfactual revenue estimator.** Using propensity logs, estimate the revenue of a candidate ranker under the current traffic distribution via IPW. This is the gate before any online experiment.
- **Online: revenue + quality guardrails.** A/B test with primary metric **RPM** and guardrail metrics **CTR**, **conversion rate**, **ad quality score**, **organic engagement** (does showing more ads tank organic clicks?). Violation of any guardrail blocks the launch.

The novelty effect (Lesson 6) applies here too — users adapt to ad placement over weeks.

### 3.5 Deploy and serve

Latency budget mirrors Lesson 5 (200ms p99 typical). Ad-specific additions:

- **Auction at the very end** of the request path, after ranking, with a sub-millisecond compute cost
- **Budget pacing** — the system must enforce daily budgets per advertiser, throttling impressions for high-spend campaigns to prevent same-day budget exhaustion
- **Frequency capping** — a single user must not see the same ad more than N times per day; this is a request-time filter on retrieved candidates

### 3.6 Monitor and maintain

Inherits Lesson 7 (drift detection, multi-tier retraining, shadow + canary, auto-rollback). Ad-specific:

- **Calibration drift** is monitored as a first-class metric — if the calibration error grows past a threshold, the calibrator is re-fit immediately, not waiting for the next full retrain
- **Auction-revenue drift** monitored continuously; sudden RPM drops trigger investigation
- **Advertiser feedback** as a signal — too many "where did my ad go?" tickets indicates a model or auction regression

## 4. Back-of-envelope math

### 4.1 The cost of miscalibration

Suppose true pCTR is 0.05 and the model predicts 0.10. With a $1.00 bid, the model treats the impression as worth `0.10 × $1.00 = $0.10`. The advertiser is willing to pay up to that. But the impression is actually worth `0.05 × $1.00 = $0.05`. Every winning auction at this miscalibration is **2× over-priced**.

If 30% of auctions are won at this miscalibration over a quarter, advertiser ROI collapses, advertisers reduce budgets, and quarterly revenue can drop by mid-single-digit percent. **Calibration is a billion-dollar concern at scale.**

### 4.2 GSP price math

In a GSP auction with positions ranked by `score = pCTR × bid`, the price the winner pays per click is:

$$
\text{price}_{\text{winner}} = \frac{\text{score}_{\text{next}}}{\text{pCTR}_{\text{winner}}}
$$

So the winner pays just enough to outbid the next ad — adjusted by their pCTR. If the next ad's score is 0.08 and the winner's pCTR is 0.10, the price per click is `$0.80`. This is the central economic identity of ad ranking.

### 4.3 Counterfactual revenue estimator

For a new ranker, IPW-estimated revenue over logged impressions:

$$
\hat{R}_{\text{new}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{\mathbb{1}[\text{new policy chose ad}_i]}{\pi_{\text{old}}(\text{ad}_i \mid \text{request}_i)} \cdot r_i
$$

where $\pi_{\text{old}}$ is the logged propensity and $r_i$ is the observed revenue. This estimator is unbiased only when the new policy's support is contained in the old (every ad the new policy picks must have been pickable under the old policy). Mature ad teams maintain this estimator as a release gate.

## 5. What the interviewer is actually grading (case-specific)

- **Did you open with `pCTR × bid` scoring, not just pCTR?** Pure pCTR is the L4 ceiling.
- **Did you name calibration as essential to the auction?** Generic "yes calibration matters" is L4; tying it to revenue is L5.
- **Did you describe the auction (GSP / VCG)?** Skipping the auction is treating ads as plain ranking.
- **Did you mention counterfactual logging and IPW off-policy evaluation?** L5+ signal.
- **Did you propose ad-quality and user-satisfaction guardrails?** Revenue-only optimisation is the canonical ad-system failure mode.
- **Did you mention budget pacing and frequency capping?** Operational maturity signals.

Failure modes to avoid:

- **Pure pCTR ranking.** No auction, no bid weighting — economically wrong.
- **No calibration.** Auction prices are nonsense without it.
- **Treating ads as recsys.** The unit is the slot, but the slot allocation is an auction, not a sort.
- **No quality guardrails.** Revenue maximisation without quality is the textbook failure mode of ad systems.
