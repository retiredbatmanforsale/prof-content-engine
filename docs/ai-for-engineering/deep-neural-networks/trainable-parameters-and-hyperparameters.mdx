<LessonHero number="7" title="Trainable parameters and hyperparameters" subtitle="What the optimizer learns vs. what you set before training begins" />

Lessons 5–6 followed **one** small network: **W₁**, **b₁**, **W₂**, **b₂**. Those symbols are **trainable parameters**—numbers **learned** from data via **gradient-based updates**. This lesson separates them from **hyperparameters**: **settings** you (or a tuner) choose **outside** the gradient loop. That distinction drives **model complexity**, **capacity**, and how we think about **scaling** **quality** with **parameter count** **n**.

---

## Trainable parameters (what backprop updates)

**Trainable parameters** are every scalar **θ** that appears in the forward map and gets a **partial derivative** **∂L/∂θ** in the backward pass, then moves in **gradient descent** (Lesson 6).

In our **3 → 4 → 2** example:

- **W₁** (input → hidden): **4 × 3** matrix → **12** weights  
- **b₁**: **4** biases (one per hidden neuron)  
- **W₂** (hidden → output): **2 × 4** matrix → **8** weights  
- **b₂**: **2** biases (one per output neuron)

**Total trainable parameters:** **12 + 4 + 8 + 2 = 26**.

### Where they “live” in the picture

- **Weights** sit on **edges** (connections): each link from an input or hidden unit to the next layer has one **multiplicative** weight.  
- **Biases** sit on **neurons** (units): they **shift** the **pre-activation** before the nonlinearity, like an extra **always-on** input with weight **1**.

So **all** trainable parameters are either **edge weights** or **neuron biases** in a fully connected stack.

### Backprop and history (one sentence)

**Backpropagation** is the **efficient** way to compute **∂L/∂θ** for **all** those weights and biases. The procedure **widely used today** was **popularized** by **Rumelhart**, **Hinton**, and **Williams** in **[Learning representations by back-propagating errors](https://www.nature.com/articles/323533a0)** (**1986**, *Nature*); **earlier related work** (e.g. **Werbos**) also developed **similar ideas**. **Hinton** remains a **central figure** in that **story** and in **deep learning** more broadly.

---

## Hyperparameters (configuration, not learned by ∂L/∂θ on the net)

**Hyperparameters** **configure** **how** the network is **built** and **trained**. They are **not** the **weights** or **biases** updated by **backprop** on the **same** loss (though **meta-learning** blurs this at advanced levels).

Typical examples:

- **Learning rate** **η** (Lesson 6)  
- **Batch size**, number of **epochs**  
- **Architecture**: **width** (units per layer), **depth** (number of layers), **choice** of **activation** functions  
- **Regularization** strength (**weight decay** / **L2** **λ**, **dropout** **rate**)  
- **Loss** **choice** when you **commit** to a **task** (e.g. **MSE** vs **cross-entropy**)

You **pick** them from **experience**, **search**, or **validation** performance—not from **∂L/∂η** inside the **standard** training loop.

## Model complexity and **parameter count** **n**

**Model complexity** (informally, **capacity**) tracks **how rich** the family of functions your architecture can represent. For a fixed **hyperparameter** blueprint—**layer sizes**, **depth**, **connectivity**—**raising** **n** (more weights and biases) generally **increases** **capacity**: the net can **fit** **more** **intricate** **patterns**.

That is a **double-edged** fact:

- With **enough** **data** and **good** **optimization**, **larger** **n** often **improves** **validation** **quality** up to a point.  
- With **too little** **data** or **weak** **regularization**, a **very** **large** **n** can **memorize** **noise** (**overfitting**).

So **complexity** is **tied** to **n**, but **good** **generalization** also depends on **data**, **hyperparameters**, and **training** **procedure**.

---

## **Scaling laws**: quality and **n** (and not only **n**)

In **large-scale** training (especially **language** and **vision** **foundation** models), **empirical** **scaling** **laws** describe how **metrics** such as **test** **loss** improve as **power laws** in **quantities** like **parameter count** **n**, **dataset** **size**, and **compute** used for training. A **rough** **intuition**:

$$
\text{quality} \;\longleftrightarrow\; f(n,\ \text{data},\ \text{compute})
$$

So **quality** of the model’s output **does depend on** **n**: larger models **can** be better when trained well. It is **not only** **n**—you need enough **data** and **compute** to use extra parameters effectively, plus sensible **hyperparameters**. **Scaling-law** curves (often **loss** vs **n** on **log–log** axes) make that relationship easy to see in large-model research.

Our **3 → 4 → 2** example has **n = 26**, which is tiny. The same split—**trainable** weights and biases **vs** **hyperparameters** that define the run—applies when **n** reaches **millions** or **billions**.

---

## One-line story

**Trainable** parameters (**weights** on **edges**, **biases** on **neurons**) update with **backprop**; **hyperparameters** set the **configuration**. Larger **n** raises **capacity** and, when **data** and **compute** scale too, often improves what the model can deliver.
