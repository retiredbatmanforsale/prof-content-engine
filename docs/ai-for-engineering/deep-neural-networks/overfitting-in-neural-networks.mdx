<LessonHero number="8" title="Overfitting in neural networks" subtitle="When your network memorizes instead of generalizing — and how to fix it" />

**Overfitting** means the network fits the **training set** too closely—including **noise** and quirks—so **validation** or **test** performance stalls or worsens while **training loss** keeps dropping. You often see a **widening gap** between **train** and **val** metrics. Lesson 7 linked **capacity** and parameter count **n** to this risk: high capacity plus limited data makes overfitting easy.

This lesson groups **mitigations** by **where** in the workflow you act: **data**, **model**, **training**, **pre-training**, and **post-training**. The vocabulary overlaps (**regularization**, **generalization**); the useful question is **what you change** and **when**.

---

## What you are trying to preserve

You want **low error on held-out data**, not just on examples the optimizer has seen many times. Everything below steers learning toward **patterns that transfer** instead of **memorization**.

---

## 1. **Data** side (before and during the project)

**When:** collecting, cleaning, and feeding examples.

- **More diverse, representative data** — often the strongest lever: the model has less reason to memorize a thin slice of reality if coverage grows and matches how the system will be used.  
- **Train / validation / test splits** — hold **validation** out for tuning; reserve **test** for a final honest check so you do not accidentally tune to the test set.  
- **Data augmentation** — for images: crops, flips, color jitter; for text: paraphrases or light noise where it makes sense. Augmentation expands effective coverage without new labels.

These levers do not shrink **n**; they change what the model is encouraged to treat as equivalent supervision.

---

## 2. **Model** side (architecture and inductive bias)

**When:** designing the network before or between training runs.

- **Reduce capacity** — smaller width, fewer layers, or fewer parameters **n** when data is scarce (Lesson 7). Simpler hypothesis classes often generalize better when the signal is weak.  
- **Weight decay** (L2 regularization) — penalize large weights in the objective so solutions stay smoother; implemented as an extra loss term or a decoupled optimizer rule.  
- **Dropout** (and related stochastic-depth ideas) — randomly zero units during training so the network cannot rely on fixed pathways; at inference you typically use the full network with rescaling. A standard model-side regularizer for dense layers.

You can combine several of these; they target different failure modes (raw capacity vs weight magnitude vs co-adaptation of units).

---

## 3. **Training** procedure (the optimization loop)

**When:** every epoch or step, and while reading learning curves.

- **Early stopping** — track **validation** loss or a task metric; stop training when validation stops improving (with a patience window). Cuts off extra epochs that mostly bake in training-set noise.  
- **Learning rate** and **schedule** — the step size shapes exploration vs convergence; warmup and decay are common ways to avoid unstable or wasteful optimization.  
- **Batch size** — smaller batches add gradient noise that sometimes helps generalization; it is one of several training knobs, not a cure-all.

**Label smoothing**, **Mixup**, and similar target or input mixing tricks also live here: they soften hard targets or synthetic combinations so the model is less tempted to memorize exact training pairs.

---

## 4. **Pre-training** (before your task-specific fit)

**When:** initialization or an upstream stage—before or above your final fine-tune.

- **Transfer learning** — start from weights trained on a large source task (e.g. ImageNet, broad text corpora). A small labeled downstream set may be enough to adapt a head if generic features are already learned.  
- **Freeze early layers at first** — train only the top layers (or blocks), then optionally unfreeze more with a smaller learning rate. That limits how many parameters move freely when data is tiny.

**Self-supervised** pre-training (contrastive learning, masked modeling, etc.) fills the same role: learn representations with less reliance on your full label budget.

---

## 5. **Post-training** (after the main training run)

**When:** you already have a trained checkpoint—ensembling, deployment, or inference-time refinements.

- **Ensembling** — average or vote over several models (different seeds, architectures, or cross-validation folds). Reduces variance; costs more compute at inference.  
- **Test-time augmentation (TTA)** — average predictions over augmented views of the same input; common in vision.  
- **Production monitoring** — watch for **distribution shift**; retrain or roll back when live traffic no longer matches training. That is not textbook overfitting, but the symptom is the same: poor fit to new conditions.

Post-training tricks rarely replace good data, model, and training choices; they polish what you already learned.

## One-line story

Overfitting is great on the training set but weak on new data; you respond with **data**, **smaller or regularized models**, **smarter training**, **pre-trained starts**, and sometimes **post-hoc ensembling**.
