## Fundamentals and Core Mechanics

<InterviewQuestion
  number={1}
  difficulty="Fundamentals"
  company={["Google", "OpenAI", "Apple"]}
  question="Derive backpropagation updates for a single hidden-layer network with sigmoid activations."
>

High-signal expectation: show chain-rule structure, not just final equations.

For output layer:
- `delta_out = (y_hat - y) * sigma'(z_out)` for MSE-style derivation
- `dW_out = a_hidden^T * delta_out`, `db_out = sum(delta_out)`

For hidden layer:
- `delta_hidden = (delta_out * W_out^T) * sigma'(z_hidden)`
- `dW_hidden = x^T * delta_hidden`, `db_hidden = sum(delta_hidden)`

If using sigmoid + BCE at output, mention simplification:
- `delta_out = (y_hat - y)` (the sigmoid derivative cancels)

Strong candidates explain where vanishing gradients appear in this chain as depth increases.

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={2}
  difficulty="Fundamentals"
  company={["Meta", "Microsoft", "Amazon", "Apple"]}
  question="Explain the bias-variance trade-off in deep neural networks. How do dropout and L2 regularization change it?"
>

- **High bias**: underfit, both train and validation errors high.
- **High variance**: overfit, train low and validation high.
- **Dropout** increases stochastic regularization; lowers variance and improves robustness, but too much can raise bias.
- **L2 regularization** penalizes large weights, smooths decision boundaries, reduces variance, and can improve calibration.

In interviews, go beyond definition and state diagnosis flow:
1) inspect learning curves,  
2) classify failure as bias vs variance,  
3) choose remedy accordingly.

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={3}
  difficulty="Fundamentals"
  company={["OpenAI", "Google", "Meta", "Microsoft"]}
  question="Explain self-attention and why the scaling factor 1/sqrt(d_k) is necessary."
>

Self-attention computes:

`Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d_k)) V`

Without scaling, dot-product magnitudes grow with `d_k`, pushing softmax into saturation where gradients are tiny and training becomes unstable.  
Scaling keeps logits in a trainable regime and stabilizes optimization in deeper transformers.

Good follow-up signal:
- distinguish self-attention vs cross-attention
- explain causal mask in decoder-only models
- mention memory/runtime cost of `O(n^2)`

</InterviewQuestion>

## Training, Optimization, and Debugging

<InterviewQuestion
  number={4}
  difficulty="Training/Debugging"
  company={["Google", "Meta", "Amazon"]}
  question="Your training loss is stuck at a high value. Walk through a systematic debugging plan."
>

A strong plan is layered and falsifiable:

1. **Data sanity**
   - Check label integrity, class ratios, leakage, and preprocessing parity.
2. **Overfit tiny subset test**
   - If model cannot overfit 100-500 examples, issue is likely implementation or architecture.
3. **Gradient flow**
   - Track gradient norms layer-wise; detect zeros/explosions.
4. **Initialization and optimizer**
   - Verify init (He/Xavier), learning rate, warmup, weight decay.
5. **Loss-activation compatibility**
   - Ensure output activation and loss are correctly paired.
6. **Instrumentation**
   - Plot train/val curves, per-batch loss, and feature/label drift.

Interviewers look for ordering, hypotheses, and quick isolation strategy, not random knob-tuning.

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={5}
  difficulty="Training/Debugging"
  company={["Apple", "Google", "OpenAI"]}
  question="Why do vanishing gradients happen, and how do residual connections help?"
>

Vanishing gradients arise when repeated multiplication by small derivatives (for example sigmoid/tanh in saturated regions) shrinks gradient magnitude exponentially with depth.

Residual blocks learn:
`y = F(x) + x`

The identity path gives a direct gradient route, reducing attenuation and making very deep networks trainable.

Common high-quality fixes to mention:
- residual/skip connections
- ReLU-family activations
- normalization (LayerNorm/BatchNorm where appropriate)
- better initialization
- gradient clipping for instability

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={6}
  difficulty="Training/Debugging"
  company={["Apple", "OpenAI", "Meta", "Google"]}
  question="How would you reduce an LLM to 4x smaller for on-device deployment with minimal quality loss?"
>

Reference answer structure:
- **Quantization** (int8/int4, sometimes lower): best latency/memory win, may hurt quality on sensitive tasks.
- **Pruning** (structured preferred for hardware): removes low-importance channels/heads.
- **Distillation**: student model learns teacher behavior, often strongest quality-retention path.
- **Serving optimizations**: KV-cache policy, operator fusion, memory layout alignment, batching strategy.

A strong senior answer compares methods under a constrained objective:
`quality floor + latency target + memory budget + power budget`.

</InterviewQuestion>

## System Design and Production Maturity

<InterviewQuestion
  number={7}
  difficulty="System Design"
  company={["Meta", "LinkedIn", "Google", "Netflix"]}
  question="Design a two-stage ranking system for a large-scale feed."
>

Expected architecture:
1. **Candidate generation** (high recall, low latency)
2. **Heavy ranker** (high precision, richer features)
3. **Re-ranker/business constraints** (diversity, freshness, safety)

Critical discussion points:
- offline vs online metric mismatch
- feedback loops and selection bias
- feature freshness and online/offline skew
- exploration vs exploitation
- guardrails (quality, policy, user harm)

Senior signal: explain why a model with better offline AUC can still reduce online CTR or retention.

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={8}
  difficulty="System Design"
  company={["Stripe", "Airbnb", "Uber", "Amazon"]}
  question="Design a real-time risk/fraud scoring system under strict latency constraints."
>

Strong blueprint:
- event ingestion (streaming), feature computation, online feature store
- low-latency inference service with fallback rules
- calibrated thresholds by risk tier
- delayed-label backfill and retraining loop
- drift and feature health monitoring

Must-cover metrics:
- precision/recall at operating threshold
- false positive business cost
- PSI/data drift and label delay sensitivity

This question primarily tests production reliability under non-stationary data.

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={9}
  difficulty="System Design"
  company={["OpenAI", "Google", "Microsoft"]}
  question="How do scaling laws influence model size, data size, and compute allocation decisions?"
>

Interview target: strategic reasoning, not equation memorization.

Good answer components:
- model quality improves predictably with more parameters/data/compute, but with diminishing returns
- there is a compute-optimal frontier; blindly scaling one axis is inefficient
- planning must include training cost, inference cost, latency SLOs, and product ROI
- evaluation strategy should detect when more compute no longer buys meaningful business lift

At senior levels, tie this to portfolio planning and launch risk.

</InterviewQuestion>

## Platform and MLOps-Oriented Signals

<InterviewQuestion
  number={10}
  difficulty="System Design"
  company={["Databricks", "Snowflake", "Microsoft", "Amazon"]}
  question="What does production-grade MLOps look like for deep neural networks?"
>

Core elements expected:
- reproducible datasets and versioned features
- experiment tracking and model registry
- CI/CD for training and serving
- shadow/canary rollout with rollback criteria
- observability: data drift, concept drift, latency, and business KPIs

Company-specific hooks from the research corpus:
- Databricks: MLflow lifecycle and registry discipline
- Snowflake: reproducible training sets and warehouse-native governance
- Microsoft/Amazon: enterprise reliability, security, and compliance constraints

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={11}
  difficulty="Training/Debugging"
  company={["Google", "Amazon", "Databricks", "Microsoft"]}
  question="What are common distributed training bottlenecks, and how do you mitigate them?"
>

Common bottlenecks:
- communication overhead (all-reduce pressure)
- load imbalance and stragglers
- memory bandwidth limits
- inefficient data pipelines (GPU starvation)
- cache-line and contention issues at scale

Mitigations:
- overlap communication/compute
- gradient accumulation and mixed precision
- better sharding strategy (data/tensor/pipeline parallel hybrids)
- input pipeline profiling and prefetch tuning
- topology-aware placement and batching

Interviewers usually score candidates on whether they can reason from hardware constraints to model throughput.

</InterviewQuestion>

<InterviewQuestion
  number={12}
  difficulty="System Design"
  company={["Apple", "Google", "OpenAI", "Meta"]}
  question="Why is LLM inference often memory-bound, and what optimizations would you prioritize first?"
>

Inference is frequently memory-bound because token generation repeatedly reads large weight matrices and KV-cache state; memory movement, not raw FLOPs, becomes dominant.

High-signal optimizations:
1. quantization for bandwidth reduction
2. KV-cache strategy and cache locality
3. kernel/operator fusion
4. continuous batching and request scheduling
5. architecture variants that reduce attention cost (where applicable)

Best answers connect these choices to latency, throughput, and quality tradeoffs, not only to benchmark speedups.

</InterviewQuestion>
