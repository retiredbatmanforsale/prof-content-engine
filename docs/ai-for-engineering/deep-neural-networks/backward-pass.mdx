## The training loop (what actually repeats)

For each **step** (one **batch** or one **example**, depending on your setup):

1. **Forward pass** вЂ” compute **zвЃЅВ№вЃѕ**, **a**, **zвЃЅВІвЃѕ**, **y** from **x** and current parameters.  
2. **Loss** вЂ” compare **y** to targets **t** (labels): **L = loss(y, t)**.  
3. **Backward pass** вЂ” compute **partial derivatives** of **L** with respect to **every** learned scalar: entries of **Wв‚Ѓ**, **bв‚Ѓ**, **Wв‚‚**, **bв‚‚**.  
4. **Update** вЂ” move parameters a little **opposite** the gradient (**gradient descent**).

Then repeat: **forward в†’ loss в†’ backward в†’ update**, again and again. Libraries hide the bookkeeping, but the **story** is always this.

## What we need from calculus

**Loss** **L** is a **scalar**. Each parameter (say an entry **(Wв‚Ѓ)в±јбµў**) influences **L** only through the **chain** of intermediate values. To get **в€‚L / в€‚(Wв‚Ѓ)в±јбµў** you:

1. Draw (or imagine) **who depends on whom**: **x** в†’ **zвЃЅВ№вЃѕ** в†’ **a** в†’ **zвЃЅВІвЃѕ** в†’ **y** в†’ **L**.  
2. Apply the **chain rule**: multiply **local derivatives** along each path, **sum** when one variable feeds **several** children (e.g. **L** may depend on **yв‚Ѓ** and **yв‚‚**; both may depend on a shared earlier node).

So **differentiation rules** for training are not mysterious: they are **sum and product of partials** along that **hierarchy**. **Backprop** is an **efficient order** to compute all those partials without redoing workвЂ”once you see the **graph**, the algorithm is **mechanical**.

- **L** depends on **y** (and **t**, but **t** is fixed dataвЂ”not a parameter).  
- **y** depends on **zвЃЅВІвЃѕ**, hence on **Wв‚‚**, **bв‚‚**, and **a**.  
- **a** depends on **zвЃЅВ№вЃѕ**, hence on **Wв‚Ѓ**, **bв‚Ѓ**, and **x**.

**Chain rule** always answers: вЂњIf I wiggle this **parent**, how does **L** move?вЂќ by threading **в€‚L/в€‚child В· в€‚child/в€‚parent** (and **adding** branches when a node has **multiple** children).

**Example (scalar intuition):** if **L** depends on **yв‚Ѓ** and **yв‚‚**, and both depend on some **u**,

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial u}
  = \frac{\partial L}{\partial y_1}\frac{\partial y_1}{\partial u}
  + \frac{\partial L}{\partial y_2}\frac{\partial y_2}{\partial u}.
$$

That **sum over paths** is the **heart** of backprop when **fan-out** happens.

## What we compute: gradients for **Wв‚Ѓ**, **bв‚Ѓ**, **Wв‚‚**, **bв‚‚**

**Training** needs **every** partial derivative of **L** with respect to **each learnable scalar**:

- **в€‚L / в€‚(Wв‚Ѓ)в±јбµў** for **j = 1..4**, **i = 1..3**  
- **в€‚L / в€‚(bв‚Ѓ)в±ј** for **j = 1..4**  
- **в€‚L / в€‚(Wв‚‚)в‚–в±ј** for **k = 1..2**, **j = 1..4**  
- **в€‚L / в€‚(bв‚‚)в‚–** for **k = 1..2**

You do **not** need to memorize closed forms for each entry by heart. You need the **pattern**:

1. Start from **в€‚L/в€‚y** (depends on your **loss**).  
2. Multiply by **в€‚y/в€‚zвЃЅВІвЃѕ** (sigmoid **derivative**).  
3. From **zвЃЅВІвЃѕ = Wв‚‚a + bв‚‚**, get sensitivities w.r.t. **Wв‚‚**, **bв‚‚**, **a**.  
4. Propagate **в€‚L/в€‚a** backward through **a = Пѓ(zвЃЅВ№вЃѕ)** to **zвЃЅВ№вЃѕ**, then through **zвЃЅВ№вЃѕ = Wв‚Ѓx + bв‚Ѓ** to **Wв‚Ѓ**, **bв‚Ѓ**.

Same **recipe** for deeper nets: **one backward step per forward op**, reusing stored **activations** from the forward pass.

## The **gradient vector** (stacking partials)

Implementation-wise, we do not keep hundreds of names like **в€‚L/в€‚(Wв‚Ѓ)в‚ѓв‚‚** separate forever. We **pack** every **в€‚L/в€‚parameter** into **one long vector** (or list of **tensors** the same shape as **Wв‚Ѓ**, **bв‚Ѓ**, вЂ¦) called the **gradient** **в€‡L** (or **в€‡Оё L** if **Оё** is вЂњall parametersвЂќ).

$$
\nabla_\theta L
  = \text{stack of all } \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_i}
$$

**Backprop** fills that structure. **Gradient descent** then uses **one** update rule on the whole lot.

---

## Update rule (gradient descent)

Let **Оё** denote **all** parameters (every entry of **Wв‚Ѓ**, **bв‚Ѓ**, **Wв‚‚**, **bв‚‚**). After computing **в€‡Оё L** on the current minibatch (or full batch):

$$
\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \, \nabla_\theta L.
$$

Here **О·** (**eta**) is the **learning rate**: a small positive **hyperparameter** that says how **large** each step is.

- **О· too small** вЂ” loss creeps down; training is **slow**.  
- **О· too large** вЂ” steps **overshoot**; loss can **oscillate** or **diverge**.  
- **Just right** вЂ” you often see **loss vs iteration** (or vs **epoch**) **curve** that **drops** quickly at first, then **flattens** as you approach a **good** region of parameter space.

So the **learning rate** directly shapes that **curve**: it is the **knob** on **how aggressively** you apply the **gradient signal** from backprop.
