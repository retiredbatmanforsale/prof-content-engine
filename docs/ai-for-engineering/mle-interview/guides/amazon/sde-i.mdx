## Process overview {#process-overview}

The Amazon SDE I loop runs **3–6 weeks** end-to-end:

1. **Online Assessment (OA)** — 70–90 min. 2 coding problems + a Work Simulation + a Workstyle Simulation
2. **Recruiter screen** — 20–30 min logistics + light fit
3. **Technical phone screen** (sometimes skipped for new grads with strong OA) — 45–60 min, 1 coding problem with follow-ups + 1–2 LP questions
4. **Onsite loop** — 4–5 rounds, 45–60 min each: 2 coding rounds + 1 system design (light, e.g. parking lot) + 1 behavioral/LP + 1 Bar Raiser

:::warning[The most common reason candidates fail Amazon]
**Behavioral fumbles, not coding.** Amazon weights Leadership Principles more heavily than any other big-tech. Fluent coding with weak LP stories will not get you an offer; the inverse sometimes will.
:::

## Leadership Principles (the actual gate) {#leadership-principles}

Amazon has **16 Leadership Principles** and explicitly maps your stories to them in the debrief. Each interviewer is assigned 2–3 LPs to probe; together they cover most of the 16 across the loop.

For SDE I, the most-probed LPs (covered in 80%+ of loops):

1. **Customer Obsession** — putting end users above your own preferences or convenience
2. **Ownership** — going beyond your assigned scope to deliver an outcome
3. **Invent and Simplify** — simplifying a complex process
4. **Learn and Be Curious** — actively learning a new technology or domain
5. **Bias for Action** — moving with incomplete information when speed matters
6. **Earn Trust** — admitting mistakes, listening, communicating clearly
7. **Dive Deep** — operating at all levels, knowing the details
8. **Deliver Results** — focusing on key inputs, finishing despite setbacks

You need **12–15 STAR stories** prepared, with each story tagged to 1–3 LPs. The interviewer asks "tell me about a time when..." and you map quickly to your prepared story for the relevant LP.

## Online assessment {#online-assessment}

<InterviewRound
  name="Online Assessment (OA)"
  duration="70–90 min total"
  format="HackerRank or Amazon platform · 4 components"
  weight="gate"
>

Four sections, completed in one sitting:

1. **2 coding problems** (~70 min) — typically one easy/medium and one medium. Common categories: arrays, strings, hash maps, two pointers, sliding window, BFS/DFS.
2. **Work Simulation** — multiple-choice scenarios about engineering tradeoffs ("you have a tight deadline and noticed a bug — what do you do?"). Map to LPs.
3. **Workstyle Simulation** — Likert-scale questions about preferences. Honest answers, but consistency matters.
4. **Approach explanations** — short text fields where you explain your solution. Don\'t skip these — they are graded.

## Technical phone screen {#technical-phone-screen}

<InterviewRound
  name="Technical Phone Screen"
  duration="45–60 min"
  format="Live with engineer · 1 coding problem + 1–2 LP questions"
  weight="gate"
>

Increasingly skipped for new grads with strong OA results, but still common. One medium coding problem with 1–2 follow-ups, then 1–2 LP behavioral questions.

The behavioral portion is **not a warm-up** — it's a real signal. Have your top-3 strongest LP stories ready (Customer Obsession, Ownership, Earn Trust are safe bets).

</InterviewRound>

## Onsite (4–5 rounds) {#onsite}

<InterviewRound name="Coding 1" duration="60 min" format="Whiteboard / virtual IDE" weight="signal">

One medium-hard problem with follow-ups. Common categories: trees, graphs, DP, intervals. Typical Amazon questions: meeting room scheduling, anagram problems, matrix rotation, BST validation, LCA.

The interviewer also asks **2–3 LP questions** in the back half of the round (~15 min). Don\'t treat this round as pure coding.

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="Coding 2" duration="60 min" format="Whiteboard / virtual IDE" weight="signal">

Same format, different interviewer, different LPs probed. Often a graph or DP problem. Recently asked: edit distance, decode ways, max product subarray, clone graph.

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="System Design (light)" duration="60 min" format="Whiteboard" weight="signal">

For SDE I, system design is **scoped low** — typical prompts are object-oriented design problems rather than full distributed systems:

- Design a parking lot
- Design a chess game
- Design a vending machine
- Design a deck-of-cards class hierarchy

Expected: requirements, class definitions, public APIs, basic database schema, edge cases. **You do not need to discuss QPS, sharding, or replication** — that\'s SDE II+ territory.

<InterviewRound name="Behavioral / Leadership Principles" duration="45–60 min" format="Conversational" weight="signal">

Pure LP round. The interviewer probes 4–5 of the 16 principles, drilling into each with 2–3 follow-ups per story.

The hardest part of this round is the **drill-down**. After your STAR story, expect:
- "What was the most challenging part?"
- "What would you do differently?"
- "How did the customer/team react?"
- "What did you learn?"
- "Have you applied that lesson since?"

Stories without specific outcomes (numbers, before/after states, named stakeholders) collapse under drill-down. **Prepare each story 3–4 levels deep**, not just the surface STAR.

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="Bar Raiser" duration="60 min" format="Conversational + technical · senior engineer outside the team" weight="veto">

The Bar Raiser is the round that most often kills offers. See the next section.

</InterviewRound>

## The Bar Raiser {#bar-raiser}

The Bar Raiser is a senior Amazon engineer **specifically trained** to maintain Amazon\'s hiring quality across teams and over time. Their veto is binding — even if your team manager wants to hire you.

Key facts:

- **You are not told which interviewer is the Bar Raiser.** Treat every round as if it is.
- The Bar Raiser is **not from your target team**. They have no incentive to lower the bar to fill the role.
- Their evaluation focuses on: **whether you would raise or lower the bar** of the team, not whether you can do the specific job.
- They probe LPs deeper than other interviewers and weight LP signal heavily.

A common pattern: 4 of 5 interviewers say "hire," but the Bar Raiser says "no hire" — the result is **no hire**. Manager advocacy does not override the Bar Raiser.

:::tip[How to recognize a Bar Raiser]
You won\'t know for sure, but tells include: not from your target team, asks deeper LP follow-ups than peers, asks "what would you do differently" repeatedly, doesn\'t talk about team-specific work. If you sense it, treat that round with extra care — but the right move is to treat *every* round that way.
:::

## Preparation plan {#preparation-plan}

LP prep dominates Amazon prep — equal weight to coding.

| Week | Focus | Daily commitment | Exit criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | LC mediums (arrays, strings, hashmaps) + LP story brainstorm | 2 mediums + 30 min stories | 14 mediums solved, 8 raw story drafts |
| 2 | LC mediums (trees, graphs, DP) + STAR refinement | 2 mediums + 30 min stories | 12 stories in STAR format |
| 3 | OOP design practice (parking lot, chess, vending machine) + drill-down rehearsal on stories | 1 design/day + mock drill-down | 5 OOP designs + 12 stories at 4 levels deep |
| 4 | Mock LP rounds + final coding mocks | 1 mock LP + 1 mock coding | 2 mock LP rounds with no story collapse |

:::tip[Story map technique]
Build a **2-column map**: 16 LPs in column A, your 12-15 stories in column B. Draw lines so every LP has at least one story. Add a "best fit" star to each line. When the interviewer asks about an LP, you go straight to your starred story without thinking. This is the single highest-leverage prep move for Amazon.
:::
