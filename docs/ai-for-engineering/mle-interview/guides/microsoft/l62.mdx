## Process overview {#process-overview}

The Microsoft L62 loop runs **4–8 weeks** end-to-end:

1. **Recruiter screen** — 30–45 min logistics + level fit
2. **Online assessment (Codility)** — 60–90 min, 2–3 coding problems
3. **Technical phone screen** (sometimes replaces OA) — 45–60 min, 1–2 medium problems
4. **Onsite loop** — 4–5 rounds: 3–4 coding rounds (medium / medium-hard) + 1 system design + behavioral woven into each round
5. **As-Appropriate (As-Ap) round** — final senior-leadership interview that can override the rest

:::warning[How Microsoft differs from FAANG]
Microsoft **weaves behavioral assessment into every technical round**, rather than dedicating a separate behavioral round. Each interviewer is checking for "growth mindset" and culture fit while you code. Treat every round as both technical and behavioral.
:::

## Microsoft leveling explained {#microsoft-leveling}

| Level | Title | Years exp | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| L59 | SDE I | 0–1 | Entry / new grad |
| L60 | SDE I | 1–2 | Junior |
| L61 | SDE II | 2–5 | Mid-level |
| **L62** | **SDE II** | **4–6** | **This guide — mid-to-senior** |
| L63 | Senior SDE | 6–10 | Senior |
| L64 | Senior SDE | 8+ | Senior / Staff start |
| L65–67 | Principal+ | 10+ | Principal level |

L62 candidates are expected to operate independently on substantial features, mentor junior engineers, and contribute to system design without being the primary driver. The interview reflects this — coding is the gate, system design is required, and behavioral signal must show ownership beyond your immediate scope.

## Online assessment {#online-assessment}

<InterviewRound
  name="Online Assessment (Codility)"
  duration="60–90 min"
  format="Codility · 2–3 problems"
  weight="gate"
>

Two or three problems on Codility. Common pattern: 1 medium + 1 medium-hard. Topics: arrays, strings, hash maps, trees, graphs, BFS/DFS, sliding window, basic DP.

The OA is a true gate — passing it grants the onsite, but a borderline pass does not survive a weak interview later. Aim to solve all problems comfortably with edge cases handled.

</InterviewRound>

## Technical phone screen {#technical-phone-screen}

<InterviewRound
  name="Technical Phone Screen"
  duration="45–60 min"
  format="Live with engineer · 1–2 medium problems · CodingPad / shared editor"
  weight="gate"
>

Sometimes substitutes for the OA, sometimes added on top. One or two medium problems with follow-ups. The interviewer also asks 1–2 behavioral questions in the back half.

Microsoft is unusually tolerant of using your most-comfortable language. They explicitly tell you to pick the language you can write fastest in.

</InterviewRound>

## Onsite (4–5 rounds) {#onsite}

<InterviewRound name="Coding 1 — DSA" duration="45–60 min" format="Whiteboard / virtual editor" weight="signal">

One medium problem with follow-ups. Common categories: trees, graphs, BFS/DFS, recursion, intervals. Recently asked: Reverse Linked List (LC 206), Word Ladder (LC 127), Course Schedule (LC 207), Binary Tree Right Side View (LC 199).

The interviewer expects you to:
1. Restate the problem and ask clarifying questions.
2. Propose an approach before coding.
3. Code with clean variable names and clear structure.
4. Dry-run on the example input.
5. Discuss complexity.

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="Coding 2 — DSA" duration="45–60 min" format="Whiteboard / virtual editor" weight="signal">

Same format, harder problem. Common: medium-hard DP, intervals, design (LRU cache, basic queue with two stacks).

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="Coding 3 — Implementation" duration="45–60 min" format="Whiteboard / virtual editor" weight="signal">

Heavier on implementation discipline than algorithmic insight. Common prompts: parsing problems, simulation, basic OOP design (vending machine, parking lot, deck of cards). The interviewer wants to see if you write production-ready code, not just clever algorithms.

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="System Design" duration="45–60 min" format="Whiteboard" weight="level">

Required at L61+. Design questions are typically targeted at **Microsoft products**:

- Design OneDrive\'s file sync system
- Design Teams chat (with persistence + presence)
- Design Azure Blob Storage
- Design SharePoint document collaboration
- Design Outlook calendar with availability search

Expected flow:

1. **Requirements** (5 min) — functional + non-functional, scale numbers.
2. **Capacity** (3–5 min) — QPS, storage, bandwidth.
3. **High-level architecture** (10–15 min) — boxes-and-arrows with named services.
4. **Deep dive** (15 min) — interviewer picks 1–2 components, often around consistency or scale.
5. **Tradeoffs** (5 min) — what breaks at 10x scale, failure modes.

**Azure service literacy** is welcome but not required. Microsoft interviewers care more about first-principles design than knowledge of specific Azure services.

<InterviewRound name="Behavioral (woven, not standalone)" duration="varies" format="Embedded in technical rounds" weight="signal">

Microsoft does **not** typically have a standalone behavioral round at L62. Instead, every interviewer asks 1–2 behavioral questions during their technical round. Cumulatively this adds up to substantial behavioral signal.

The dominant behavioral theme is **growth mindset** — Microsoft\'s explicit cultural value, derived from Carol Dweck\'s framework. Stories about learning from failure, receiving feedback well, and developing skills you didn\'t have score highly. Defensive responses to setbacks are a hard red flag.

Other themes:

- **Customer-first orientation** — choosing user-aligned tradeoffs
- **Collaboration across team boundaries** — working with PMs, designers, partner teams
- **Responsible AI** (newly emphasized) — for AI/Copilot-adjacent teams, expect questions about how you reason about model risks, fairness, and enterprise-safe AI

## The As-Appropriate (As-Ap) round {#as-appropriate}

The As-Ap is Microsoft\'s analog to Amazon\'s Bar Raiser, with a twist: it\'s **invitation-only** based on your earlier rounds.

- **You only get an As-Ap if your earlier rounds went well.** Receiving the invitation is itself a strong positive signal.
- The As-Ap is a senior leader (often a partner-level engineer or hiring manager) who can **override the rest of the loop in either direction** — they can downlevel a strong loop or save a weak one.
- The format depends on what gaps the loop revealed: they can ask coding, system design, or behavioral. Sometimes the round is half-technical, half "selling" you on the team.
- The As-Ap and the hiring manager **jointly make the final hire decision**.

If you get to As-Ap with a strong loop, the round often becomes a two-way conversation about the team\'s work. Don\'t treat it as a victory lap — but it\'s a less adversarial round than a Bar Raiser.

## Preparation plan {#preparation-plan}

| Week | Focus | Daily commitment | Exit criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | LC mediums (Microsoft-tagged) | 2 mediums/day | 14 mediums solved cleanly |
| 2 | LC mediums + selective hards | 2 mediums + 1 hard/day | LC 297, 146, 199, 207 solved |
| 3 | System design fundamentals (storage, replication, sharding, queues) | 1 design walkthrough/day | 5 designs at L62 scope |
| 4 | System design — Microsoft-product flavored (OneDrive, Teams, Outlook) | 1 mock design/day | 5 product-flavored designs |
| 5 | Growth-mindset story building + Microsoft-product familiarity | 30 min/day | 6 STAR stories with feedback/learning angles |
| 6 | Mock loops + final coding rotation | 1 mock loop/week | 2 full mocks |

:::tip[Microsoft-specific signals]
**"What\'s your favorite Microsoft product?"** is asked unironically and weighted. Have a specific answer that shows you actually use the product, with one feature you appreciate and one you\'d improve. Generic answers ("Office because it\'s ubiquitous") flag.

**For AI/Copilot teams**, expect Responsible AI questions: "How would you reason about content moderation in a Copilot suggestion?" or "How would you handle a model giving advice that could harm an enterprise customer?" These are signal rounds, not casual chat.
:::
