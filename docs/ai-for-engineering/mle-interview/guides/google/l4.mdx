## Process overview {#process-overview}

The Google L4 loop runs **~2 months** end-to-end (range: a few weeks to 3+ months depending on team match):

1. **Google Hiring Assessment (GHA)** — 30–45 min online personality + situational test
2. **Recruiter phone screen** — 30 min
3. **Technical phone screen** — 45–60 min, 1–2 medium coding problems on the VIP platform
4. **Onsite loop** — 4–5 rounds: 2–3 coding + 1 Googleyness/behavioral. **System design is optional** and depends on the team you're matching to.
5. **Hiring Committee review** — your packet is decided by senior Googlers who never met you
6. **Team match** — if hired, matched to a team after committee approval

:::warning[2025-2026 shift to in-person]
Google has been moving onsite coding rounds back to **in-person** since 2025, citing AI-assisted cheating in virtual interviews. Many candidates now do a hybrid loop: virtual phone screen + Googleyness, then fly in for onsite coding. AI assistants are explicitly prohibited and have been since 2023.
:::

## How it differs from L3 {#how-it-differs-from-l3}

- **Coding bar is harder.** L4 problems are intentionally tougher than the phone screen, with the expectation that you solve them **completely** (not just describe an approach).
- **Self-direction is expected.** Behavioral stories should show you driving work without explicit assignment, not just "my manager asked me to."
- **System design may appear** for some teams (infra, backend) — at L3 it never does.
- **Hiring Committee weighting differs.** At L4, the committee places **most weight on coding** when deciding hire vs. downlevel to L3.

## Google Hiring Assessment (GHA) {#google-hiring-assessment}

<InterviewRound
  name="Google Hiring Assessment (GHA)"
  duration="30–45 min"
  format="Online · self-paced · ~50 questions"
  weight="gate"
>

A personality + situational-judgment test. ~50 questions, mostly Likert-scale (strongly agree → strongly disagree) with some "what would you do if..." scenarios.

**Failure disqualifies you for 6 months** across all Google roles, so don't take it casually. Be honest — there's no key, but consistency matters: contradictory answers across paired questions flag you.

The questions probe alignment with Google's values: collaboration, user focus, ethical reasoning, comfort with ambiguity.

</InterviewRound>

## Technical phone screen {#technical-phone-screen}

<InterviewRound
  name="Technical Phone Screen"
  duration="45–60 min"
  format="Google Meet · VIP platform · 1–2 medium problems · no code execution · no AI"
  weight="gate"
>

Google's coding interviews use the **VIP platform** (their internal CoderPad equivalent) — plain text, no execution, no autocomplete. Practice without an IDE before this round.

The interviewer expects:
1. Clarifying questions about the problem (3–5 min).
2. A brute-force approach proposed and rejected before coding the optimized version.
3. Syntactically correct code on the first pass — typos count against you because there's no compiler to save you.
4. Manual trace through the example input.
5. Time and space complexity for both your initial and optimized solutions.

## Onsite (4–5 rounds) {#onsite}

<InterviewRound name="Coding 1" duration="45 min" format="VIP platform · in-person or Google Meet" weight="signal">

One harder problem with possible follow-ups. Common categories: graphs, trees, DP, recursion, intervals. Recently asked: Number of Unequal Triplets (LC 2475), Count Islands in Binary Trees, Merge Intervals (LC 56), Pour Water (LC 755), Best Meeting Point (LC 296).

The follow-up usually generalizes the original (e.g., "now what if there are k arrays instead of 2") or stresses scale ("now imagine the input doesn\'t fit in memory").

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="Coding 2" duration="45 min" format="VIP platform" weight="signal">

Same format, different interviewer. Often skews toward implementation discipline (string parsing, simulation, design problems like LRU cache or rate limiter) rather than pure algorithmic insight.

The two coding rounds are calibrated to give independent signals: a strong/strong is a clear hire; strong/weak goes to debrief discussion.

</InterviewRound>

<InterviewRound name="Coding 3 OR System Design" duration="45 min" format="VIP platform / whiteboard" weight="signal">

Whether you get a third coding round or a system design round **depends on the team**. Backend, infra, and platform teams typically include system design even at L4. Frontend, mobile, and applied-ML teams often skip it for an extra coding round.

If it's system design, expect a scoped product or component (URL shortener, typeahead, parking lot system) — less depth than L5 system design, but you should still hit the basics: requirements, capacity, schema, sharding, caching.

<InterviewRound name="Googleyness & Leadership" duration="45 min" format="Conversational" weight="signal">

Google evaluates against **six Googleyness attributes**:

1. **Thriving in ambiguity** — leading work when requirements are unclear
2. **Valuing feedback** — receiving and acting on criticism
3. **Effectively challenging the status quo** — pushing back constructively
4. **Putting the user first** — choosing the user-aligned tradeoff
5. **Doing the right thing** — ethical reasoning
6. **Caring about the team** — collaboration, mentorship, credit-sharing

For L4, examples can come from your last 1–2 years of work — internships and school projects are too thin. Stories should show **self-directed action**, not just task execution.

## Hiring Committee {#hiring-committee}

After the onsite, your interviewers' written feedback (the "packet") goes to a Hiring Committee — 4–6 senior Googlers across the company who **have not met you**. They vote hire/no-hire and assign a level based on the packet alone.

This means:

- **Speak so your interviewer can write strong evidence into the packet.** Vague answers produce vague write-ups, which the committee discounts. Be specific with names, numbers, and outcomes.
- **The committee weights coding most at L4.** Strong coding + average behavioral usually clears the bar. Average coding + strong behavioral usually does not.
- **Manager advocacy doesn\'t exist.** Unlike Meta or Amazon, your future manager has no influence over the hire decision.

In EU offices the order is sometimes reversed (team match first, then committee), but the committee still has final say.

## Preparation plan {#preparation-plan}

| Week | Focus | Daily commitment | Exit criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | LC mediums on plain text (no IDE) | 2 mediums/day | 14 mediums solved cleanly without execution |
| 2 | LC mediums + harder Google-tagged | 2 mediums + 1 hard/day | LC 56, 296, 2475, 755 solved |
| 3 | System design fundamentals (storage, caching, sharding) — only if your team includes SD | 1 design walkthrough/day | 5 designs at the L4 level: URL shortener, typeahead, rate limiter, parking lot, news feed |
| 4 | Googleyness stories (the 6 attributes) | 30 min stories + 1 mock | 5 STAR stories, 1 per Googleyness attribute |
| 5 | Mock loops with calibrated interviewers | 1 full mock/week | 2 mocks with no flagged round |

:::tip[Google-specific gotchas]
**Plain-text typing matters.** Practice writing Python or Java in a Google Doc with no autocomplete. The friction is real and visible to interviewers.

**Don\'t over-prepare for system design at L4.** Most L4 loops don\'t include it. Confirm with your recruiter; if they say "team is product-facing," skip the SD prep and double down on coding.
:::
